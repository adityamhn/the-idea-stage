## 1. Compliance-grade decision-and-reasoning evidence layer for high-risk agents (EU AI Act Art. 12, built for agents not models)

- **Problem.** A bank or insurer running an autonomous agent in a high-risk
  workflow (credit decisioning, claims, hiring) must, under EU AI Act Art. 12,
  keep automatic event logs over the system lifetime and retain them ≥6 months,
  and be able to *reconstruct after the fact* why each decision was made. Today
  most agent stacks emit unstructured traces (or nothing audit-grade), so the
  deployer cannot demonstrate human oversight when a regulator asks. This bites
  on essentially every high-risk decision the agent makes.
- **Why unsolved today.** Current observability tools (Langfuse, etc.) are built
  for *developers debugging*, not for *producing legally defensible evidence*:
  tamper-evident, schema-stable across model swaps, mapped to specific Annex/Article
  obligations, queryable by a compliance officer. Workaround today is bolting a
  data-warehouse + manual screenshots + a GRC consultant — expensive and unreliable.
- **Why 2028–2029.** *Signal:* Annex III high-risk obligations carry an enforcement
  deadline of Aug 2, 2026, with penalties up to €15M / 3% of global turnover, and
  Article 12 explicitly mandates lifetime logging + ≥6-month retention. *Speculation:*
  first audits and enforcement actions land in 2027–2028; that is when "we have logs"
  becomes "our logs survive an auditor," and budget moves from legal to a dedicated
  tooling line. The 2028 buying wave is the bet, not the 2026 deadline itself.
- **Funding / market signal.** EU AI Act Annex III deadline and €15M/3% penalty
  structure are confirmed (Holland & Knight; Raconteur). A nascent vendor category
  ("AI agent audit trail," governance countdown tools like FireTail) already exists,
  validating demand but not yet agent-native (Waxell; getaiactready.eu).
- **How it makes money.** B2B SaaS, seat + volume (per-decision-logged) pricing,
  sold founder-led to Heads of AI Risk / CISOs at EU-exposed banks, insurers, HR-tech.
  OSS SDK for the logging instrumentation → paid evidence store, retention, and
  auditor-export. Lands naturally in fintech where buyer trust matters.
- **My edge.** Structured-output / schema-repair SDK work and observability
  (Langfuse) experience is exactly the substrate; fintech buyer credibility
  (HDFC, PhonePe, Groww) shortcuts the design-partner conversation.
- **Strongest counterargument.** This may collapse into a feature of the agent
  platforms (LangChain/LangSmith, the model vendors) or get swallowed by incumbent
  GRC suites (OneTrust, Credo, Vanta) that already own the compliance buyer and
  can add agent logging faster than a startup can win that buyer.
- **Scores.** unsolvedness **4** (agent-native, audit-grade is genuinely missing) ·
  timing **4** (deadline now, enforcement wave 2027–28) · funding momentum **3**
  (governance funded, agent-native not yet) · monetization clarity **4** (regulatory
  must-have, clear buyer) · founder fit **4** (schema/observability + fintech) ·
  defensibility **2** (GRC incumbents + platform encroachment).

## 2. Adversarial simulation environments for pre-deployment agent validation (the "flight simulator," not the gate)

- **Problem.** A team shipping a long-horizon agent can't trust it until they've
  seen it fail safely thousands of times against realistic, adversarial, multi-step
  scenarios — but building those synthetic worlds (fake APIs, injected faults,
  shifting goals, hostile inputs) is harder than building the agent. Today they
  test against a handful of hand-written happy-path cases and discover edge-case
  failures in production.
- **Why unsolved today.** Generating *high-fidelity, adversarial* environments —
  not just isolation sandboxes — is the hard part. Existing "sandbox" tooling
  (Northflank microVMs, gVisor, Jentic) solves *containment*, not *scenario
  generation and coverage*. Workaround is engineers manually scripting mock servers
  and a few failure injections, with no coverage measurement.
- **Why 2028–2029.** *Signal:* 2026 buyers are already told to "simulate real
  conditions with synthetic data and run thousands of iterations to expose edge
  cases" (Openxcell), and regulators are building synthetic-data sandboxes (FCA
  Supercharged Sandbox, 2nd cohort 2026). *Speculation:* as agents move into
  high-stakes workflows, "show me your simulation coverage" becomes a procurement
  and insurance prerequisite by 2028, the same way fuzzing became table stakes in
  security. The coverage-as-requirement shift is the bet.
- **Funding / market signal.** Sandbox/synthetic-env tooling is an active 2026
  category (Northflank, Jentic, Adopt AI, Fastio); regulators funding synthetic
  datasets (FCA). Demand is observed; an adversarial-scenario-generation layer on
  top is not yet a named product.
- **How it makes money.** Usage-based (compute + scenario-runs) + enterprise seats;
  OSS scenario-DSL and harness → paid hosted environments, adversarial scenario
  packs by vertical, and coverage reporting. Sold to agent platform teams and
  AI-heavy enterprises.
- **My edge.** This is the sharpest match to the profile: 50+ hour unsupervised
  runs, error-recovery, evals/benchmarking (promptfoo), and offensive-security
  instinct for *adversarial* scenario design — exactly what generic sandbox vendors lack.
- **Strongest counterargument.** Simulation fidelity is a bottomless pit: the most
  valuable scenarios are domain-specific, so a horizontal platform may be too
  shallow to be trusted and too broad to go deep, while each vertical's incumbent
  builds its own sim. Timing may also be early — many teams still won't pay for
  testing rigor in 2026–27.
- **Scores.** unsolvedness **4** · timing **3** (early; could be 2028 not 2026) ·
  funding momentum **3** (sandboxes funded, adversarial-gen not) · monetization
  clarity **3** (usage model clear, willingness-to-pay unproven early) · founder
  fit **5** (dead-center of the edge) · defensibility **3** (depth-per-vertical
  is the moat, also the trap).

## 3. Real-time output-policing guardrail for regulated *voice* agents (the agentic auditor for spoken, irreversible answers)

- **Problem.** A patient-facing or customer-facing voice agent in healthcare,
  insurance, or finance speaks an answer once, in real time, with no edit button —
  and if it hallucinates dosing, coverage, or advice, the company is legally on the
  hook as if a human agent said it. The pain is the *latency-bound, irreversible,
  spoken* nature: you can't post-hoc review what's already been said aloud.
- **Why unsolved today.** Text-RAG guardrails assume you can inspect/rewrite output
  before sending; voice adds a hard real-time budget (sub-second) and turn-taking
  constraints that text-guardrail vendors don't handle. Workaround is enterprises
  *abandoning* full autonomy for "AI-assisted, human-led" — i.e., they gave up on
  the autonomous voice agent because nothing polices it fast enough.
- **Why 2028–2029.** *Signal:* ECRI ranked misuse of AI chatbots the #1 health-tech
  hazard for 2026; courts treat deployed chatbots as the company's "electronic
  agent"; the FCC/CFPB reject "the AI hallucinated" as a defense (Hayhurst; The
  Bulldog Law). 1,450+ logged AI-hallucination legal cases and rising. *Speculation:*
  by 2028 voice agents are widespread in regulated front-lines and the liability
  precedent forces a real-time-guardrail line item. The liability-forcing-function
  is the bet.
- **Funding / market signal.** Regulatory surface is concrete (TCPA/BIPA/HIPAA
  voice-AI rules — Softcery; ECRI #1 hazard). Litigation volume is observed
  (Charlotin's database, 1,450 cases). Voice-specific real-time policing is not yet
  a funded category — mostly text guardrails.
- **How it makes money.** Per-minute / per-call usage pricing layered into voice-agent
  platforms; sold to regulated voice deployers and to the voice-agent platforms
  themselves as an embeddable safety SDK. OSS latency-optimized policy engine →
  paid managed service + vertical policy packs.
- **My edge.** Low-latency multi-provider LLM routing, structured-output/schema-repair,
  and reliability-under-adversarial-conditions are the core of a real-time policer;
  fintech/regulated buyer credibility helps land design partners.
- **Strongest counterargument.** The voice-agent platforms (Retell, Vapi, ElevenLabs,
  the telephony+LLM stacks) will treat real-time guardrails as core and build them
  in-house, since latency coupling makes a third-party hop costly — leaving a thin
  wedge. And "human-in-the-loop" may simply remain the accepted safe answer,
  shrinking the autonomous market this depends on.
- **Scores.** unsolvedness **4** (voice real-time is a real gap) · timing **3**
  (liability building, autonomy adoption uncertain) · funding momentum **2** (voice
  guardrails not yet a funded line) · monetization clarity **4** (per-call, clear) ·
  founder fit **3** (reliability/latency yes, voice domain new) · defensibility **2**
  (platforms can absorb it).

## 4. Behavioral-equivalence verification for AI-driven legacy code migration (prove the COBOL→cloud rewrite didn't change behavior)

- **Problem.** Banks and governments are now using agentic tools to migrate COBOL/
  mainframe systems to modern stacks — but the migration agent has no trustworthy
  way to prove the new code does *exactly* what the old code did, including the
  undocumented edge cases the business secretly depends on. A single silent
  behavioral divergence in a core banking ledger is catastrophic. The unsolved part
  is *verification*, not translation.
- **Why unsolved today.** The migration vendors (AWS Transform, IBM watsonx Code
  Assistant for Z, Microsoft CAMF) automate *translation*; everyone admits "human
  experts must validate business logic." That validation is manual, slow, and the
  bottleneck. No autonomous system generates differential tests, mines real input
  distributions, and produces an equivalence dossier the risk committee trusts.
- **Why 2028–2029.** *Signal:* 2026 is the inflection — Anthropic's COBOL playbook
  in Claude Code (Feb 2026) reportedly knocked IBM stock 7.4% (FinancialContent);
  AWS/IBM/Microsoft all shipped agentic migration in 2026. *Speculation:* the wave
  of started migrations hits the *cutover* phase in 2028–29, where verification is
  the gating risk and the spend concentrates. Migration creates a verification
  market on a lag — that lag is the bet.
- **Funding / market signal.** Heavy incumbent investment in agentic migration
  itself is confirmed (AWS, IBM, Microsoft, Deluxe Corp deployment; Domain-b).
  That validates the migration market; the *verification* sub-market is the
  under-served adjacency.
- **How it makes money.** High-ticket enterprise engagements + per-migration
  verification platform license; usage-based on tests generated / systems verified.
  Sold founder-led to banks/government modernization programs and to the SIs
  (Accenture/EY/Deloitte) running them, as the trust layer on top of their migration.
- **My edge.** Adversarial test-generation, long-horizon autonomous execution, and
  fintech/government buyer credibility (HDFC, Gov of India) map directly; verification
  is fundamentally an offensive-testing problem (find the input where they diverge).
- **Strongest counterargument.** The migration platforms will fold verification into
  their own pipeline (it's the obvious upsell and they own the customer and the
  codebase context), and SIs may keep verification as billable human work they don't
  want disrupted. A standalone verifier may be squeezed between both.
- **Scores.** unsolvedness **4** (verification genuinely lags translation) · timing
  **3** (cutover wave is 2028+, slightly early to sell now) · funding momentum **4**
  (migration heavily funded; adjacency rides it) · monetization clarity **4**
  (high-ticket, clear buyer) · founder fit **4** (adversarial testing + regulated
  buyers) · defensibility **3** (incumbent upsell risk, but verification independence
  is itself a selling point).

## 5. Merchant-side fraud & malfunction detection for agentic commerce (defending against rogue and exploited buying agents)

- **Problem.** As AI agents start transacting on behalf of consumers, merchants
  lose the human behavioral signals (mouse movement, hesitation, engagement) that
  fraud systems rely on, and risk concentrates in the authentication/integration
  layer. Merchants can't tell a legitimate delegated agent from a compromised one,
  an abusive scraper, or a malfunctioning agent looping purchases — and "the agent
  did it" is nobody's accepted excuse.
- **Why unsolved today.** Fraud stacks are tuned to human telemetry; agent traffic
  breaks those models. The new protocols (Google UCP, AP2, Mastercard Verifiable
  Intent, Akamai Trusted Agent Protocol) standardize *intent and identity* but not
  *merchant-side anomaly detection over agent behavior*. Workaround: merchants
  either block agent traffic outright or wave it through blind.
- **Why 2028–2029.** *Signal:* Visa, Mastercard, Google all shipped agentic-commerce
  trust primitives in 2026 (Visa newsroom; Mastercard); a WEF-cited estimate says
  by 2028 one in four data breaches could stem from AI-agent exploitation (Unit 42 /
  Hogan Lovells). *Speculation:* volume of agentic checkout becomes material by
  2028–29, and the first wave of agent-driven fraud/abuse creates the detection
  market. The fraud-volume-follows-adoption curve is the bet.
- **Funding / market signal.** Protocol-layer investment is confirmed and dated
  (Visa Jan 2026; Google UCP Jan 2026; Mastercard Verifiable Intent 2026). The
  detection/scoring layer on top of these protocols is the open adjacency.
- **How it makes money.** Usage-based per-transaction-scored + enterprise platform
  fee; sold to merchants, PSPs, and fraud-stack vendors as an agent-behavior risk
  API. OSS agent-traffic detection signatures → paid real-time scoring + case tooling.
- **My edge.** Offensive-security depth (how agents get exploited/abused) plus
  fintech buyer credibility (PhonePe, Groww, MakeMyTrip-style commerce) and
  adversarial-behavior modeling — a genuinely security-shaped problem.
- **Strongest counterargument.** The card networks and incumbent fraud platforms
  (Visa, Mastercard, Stripe Radar, Sift, Forter) own the transaction flow, the data,
  and the merchant relationship, and are *already* building agent-trust into the
  rails — a startup may never get enough cross-merchant signal to compete on
  detection quality.
- **Scores.** unsolvedness **3** (real gap, but networks moving fast) · timing **3**
  (fraud volume is 2028+) · funding momentum **4** (commerce protocols heavily
  backed) · monetization clarity **4** (per-transaction, proven model shape) ·
  founder fit **3** (security+fintech yes, but data-network disadvantage) ·
  defensibility **2** (incumbents own the data network effect).
