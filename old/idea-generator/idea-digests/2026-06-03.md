# Idea scout - 2026-06-03

## Step 2 forecast - the AI-shaped world of 2028-2029

**Observed signal.** The market has shifted from "AI as feature" to "AI as execution layer." YC's Summer 2026 RFS says AI is now the foundation for rebuilding software, services, and silicon, and explicitly asks for AI-native service companies, company brains, software for agents, dynamic interfaces, personalized medicine, discovery engines, hardware supply chain, and inference chips for agent workflows ([YC RFS, Summer 2026](https://www.ycombinator.com/rfs?year=2025)). Sequoia's 2026 thesis says long-horizon agents have crossed from talkers to doers and that selling the work, not the tool, is the safer way to avoid model-platform commoditization ([Sequoia, "2026: This is AGI"](https://sequoiacap.com/article/2026-this-is-agi/), [Sequoia, "Services: The New Software"](https://sequoiacap.com/article/services-the-new-software/)). Bessemer is saying the same in health AI: AI-services-as-software can deliver service outcomes with software-like margins when humans handle exceptions, not the core work ([Bessemer State of Health AI 2026](https://www.bvp.com/atlas/state-of-health-ai-2026)).

**Observed signal.** Frontier deployment is becoming operational, not hypothetical. OpenAI launched workspace agents for business workflows in April 2026 ([OpenAI](https://openai.com/index/introducing-workspace-agents-in-chatgpt//)); Google I/O 2026 emphasized managed agents and "prompts to action" with Gemini 3.5 Flash ([Google](https://blog.google/innovation-and-ai/technology/developers-tools/google-io-2026-developer-highlights/)); Anthropic's enterprise-agent push included finance, engineering, design plug-ins, controlled data flows, private marketplaces, and custom plug-ins ([TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/02/24/anthropic-launches-new-push-for-enterprise-agents-with-plugins-for-finance-engineering-and-design/)). At the same time, capital is concentrating around frontier labs and AI infrastructure: Anthropic's latest reported $65B round at a $965B valuation and OpenAI's reported $110B round at a $730B valuation are not normal software rounds; they are evidence that compute, deployment, and enterprise distribution are now strategic infrastructure ([Axios](https://www.axios.com/2026/05/28/anthropic-ai-fundraising-openai), [Tom's Hardware](https://www.tomshardware.com/tech-industry/artificial-intelligence/openai-raises-110-billion-in-largest-ever-private-tech-funding-round)).

**Speculative bet.** By 2028-2029, many companies will not "use AI tools"; they will run semi-autonomous workforces over company systems, customer channels, lab equipment, financial workflows, industrial operations, and web transactions. The winners are unlikely to be generic eval dashboards or thin wrappers over model outputs. The better bet is narrow ownership of a paid workflow where the buyer already outsources labor, where records and liability matter, and where every model improvement lowers delivery cost.

**Derived problem shape.** The new gaps are not only "make agents smarter." They are: prove AI-mediated work was done correctly; translate messy human systems into machine-executable interfaces; keep regulators, auditors, insurers, and customers comfortable with autonomous execution; and package specialist workflows as outcomes. I avoided prior digests and reports covering generic agent evals, MCP scanning, agent red-team/RCA, high-risk AI audit logs, content provenance, customs brokerage, interconnection studies, stablecoin exception ops, freight detention recovery, public-records redaction, construction docs, AI-crawler revenue, and broad AI insurance underwriting.

## The 10 ideas

### 1. Post-quantum cryptography migration evidence desk

**Problem.** CISOs at critical-infrastructure operators and regulated SaaS vendors need to inventory, prioritize, migrate, and prove every externally exposed cryptographic dependency before customer and regulator deadlines, but most have partial asset maps and no audit-ready migration packet.

**Industry & who pays.** Cybersecurity / critical infrastructure; CISO, head of infrastructure security, federal-program compliance owner.

**Why unsolved today.** PQC inventory tools can find some certificates and TLS endpoints, but the hard work is dependency analysis across vendors, embedded devices, protocols, product firmware, third-party SDKs, contracts, and customer attestations. Today this is consulting plus spreadsheets.

**Why 2028-2029.** Observed: NIST's NCCoE PQC migration project is active and CISA/NSA/NIST have urged organizations, especially critical infrastructure, to prepare now ([NIST NCCoE FAQ](https://pages.nist.gov/nccoe-migration-post-quantum-cryptography/), [CISA](https://www.cisa.gov/news-events/news/cisa-nsa-and-nist-publish-new-resource-migrating-post-quantum-cryptography)). Observed: crypto/PQC startup funding is already moving, including Project Eleven's $20M Series A for post-quantum blockchain security ([The Block](https://www.theblock.co/post/385583/project-eleven-series-a-funding-crypto-quantum-threats/)). Speculation: by 2028, customer security questionnaires and cyber insurers will ask for demonstrable PQC transition evidence, not policy statements.

**VC / funding / market signal.** Security budgets already buy compliance evidence; PQC has both policy urgency and startup funding. The service-as-software shape also fits Sequoia's "sell the work" thesis.

**How it makes money.** Annual migration program fee plus per-system evidence packets; land with inventory and board roadmap, expand into remediation tracking and customer attestation portal. Service-as-software.

**Strongest counterargument.** This may be captured by existing PKI vendors, cloud providers, and security consultancies; if migration tooling becomes a bundled feature inside DigiCert, AWS, Microsoft, or Wiz, the startup is left with low-margin services.

**Fit note.** Plausible fit: cybersecurity, infra, and compliance evidence are close to your stated interests.

**Scores.** unsolvedness 4 - the evidence/migration work remains messy; timing 4 - standards are here but completion windows are long; funding momentum 4 - quantum security is getting funded; monetization clarity 5 - CISOs already pay for compliance programs; market budget 5 - critical infrastructure and enterprise security budgets are real; defensibility-vs-foundation-labs 4 - labs will not own crypto dependency migration.

### 2. AI-assisted proxy-vote fiduciary operations desk

**Problem.** Registered investment advisers voting thousands of proxies per season need issue research, client-policy mapping, conflict checks, vote rationale, and books-and-records evidence for every material vote, but proxy-advisor reliance is under scrutiny and AI-generated recommendations are not yet operationally defensible.

**Industry & who pays.** Asset management / financial compliance; chief compliance officer, stewardship head, investment operations.

**Why unsolved today.** ISS/Glass Lewis provide recommendations, and large firms can build internal AI, but mid-market advisers lack a workflow that ties fund policy, issuer materials, engagement history, AI memo, human review, conflicts, and final vote into one reconstructable record.

**Why 2028-2029.** Observed: SEC Division of Investment Management Director Brian Daly said AI and agentic AI present an opportunity for proxy voting at portfolio scale ([Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-09/sec-official-says-investment-advisors-can-use-ai-for-proxy-votes)); legal commentary says fiduciary duties still govern and AI-assisted proxy analysis requires reviewability ([Dechert](https://www.dechert.com/knowledge/onpoint/2026/1/sec-im-director-shares-views-on-proxy-voting.html)). Speculation: by 2028, proxy season will be too large and politically sensitive for advisers to outsource blindly or run AI informally.

**VC / funding / market signal.** ACA's May 2026 survey found 84% of financial-services firms use AI but fewer than one in five compliance functions have practical deployment, leaving budget without embedded tools ([ACA Group](https://www.acaglobal.com/news-and-announcements/ai-use-in-financial-services-compliance-and-operations-is-widespread-but-shallow-aca-group-survey-finds/)).

**How it makes money.** Per-AUM or per-vote-season managed workflow; premium for contested votes, custom policies, and audit-defense packets. Service-as-software.

**Strongest counterargument.** Proxy advisory firms, custodians, and large asset managers can internalize this. The market may be too narrow unless it expands from proxy voting into broader stewardship operations.

**Fit note.** Strong fit: finance infra plus AI governance, with clear regulated workflow.

**Scores.** unsolvedness 4 - AI memo generation exists, fiduciary workflow is immature; timing 5 - SEC comments and proxy-advisor scrutiny create a window; funding momentum 3 - vertical fintech/compliance signal, not many pure rounds; monetization clarity 4 - seasonal but high-stakes; market budget 4 - asset managers pay for proxy ops; defensibility-vs-foundation-labs 4 - domain records and policy workflows matter more than raw model quality.

### 3. Synthetic-claim evidence triage for P&C insurers

**Problem.** P&C claims teams receiving photo, receipt, audio, and video evidence need to separate legitimate claims from AI-inflated or fabricated evidence within hours, but detectors alone cannot produce the fraud referral, reserve-impact, and litigation-ready casefile adjusters need.

**Industry & who pays.** Insurance; head of claims, SIU leader, fraud analytics owner.

**Why unsolved today.** Deepfake/image detectors are point tools. Adjusters still need provenance checks, policy interpretation, claimant communication, repair-estimate comparison, prior-claim linkage, law-enforcement referral thresholds, and state fraud-bureau reporting.

**Why 2028-2029.** Observed: Debevoise warned in January 2026 that AI-generated images create hard insurance decisions around inflated but partially legitimate claims ([Debevoise](https://www.debevoise.com/insights/publications/2026/01/use-of-ai-generated-images-for-fake-insurance)). SAS warned in May 2026 that fraudsters can create or alter claim images in seconds ([Insurance Business](https://www.insurancebusinessmag.com/us/news/cyber/warning-over-new-ai-insurance-scams-577092.aspx)). ACFE/SAS found 55% of fraud fighters expect deepfake social engineering and GenAI document fraud to increase significantly over 24 months ([ACFE](https://www.acfe.com/about-the-acfe/newsroom-for-media/press-releases/press-release-detail?s=2026-anti-fraud-technology-benchmarking-report-pr)). Speculation: by 2028, low-dollar claims will be attacked at scale and carriers will need automated casefile assembly, not only detection.

**VC / funding / market signal.** Insurtech AI adoption and AI-risk coverage are active, but this is not the prior "AI-exposure underwriting" idea; it is claims operations.

**How it makes money.** Per-claim triage fee plus recovered-loss share for high-confidence fraud; enterprise annual license for carriers. Service-as-software.

**Strongest counterargument.** Verisk, CCC, Mitchell, Guidewire, and fraud analytics incumbents already sit in the claims workflow and can add synthetic-media modules.

**Fit note.** Plausible but less direct: AI fraud/security angle fits; insurance workflow expertise is the gap.

**Scores.** unsolvedness 4 - detectors do not equal casefile resolution; timing 5 - image generation quality is crossing the fraud threshold now; funding momentum 3 - insurtech is mixed but fraud budgets are durable; monetization clarity 5 - loss avoidance is measurable; market budget 5 - claims leakage is a real P&L line; defensibility-vs-foundation-labs 4 - labs will not own insurer SIU workflow.

### 4. N-of-1 therapy regulatory and CMC packet operator

**Problem.** Rare-disease clinicians and small biotechs trying to develop individualized RNA or genome-editing therapies need to assemble plausible-mechanism, safety, CMC, master-protocol, and patient-specific evidence packages fast enough to matter clinically, but the workflow is too bespoke for standard regulatory software.

**Industry & who pays.** Life sciences / precision medicine; rare-disease biotech, academic medical center, patient foundation, pharma rare-disease group.

**Why unsolved today.** Regulatory writing tools and eTMF systems assume repeatable trials and product lines. N-of-1 therapy requires linking mutation biology, guide/oligo design, manufacturing release, patient eligibility, safety comparators, ethics review, and FDA correspondence into one defensible dossier.

**Why 2028-2029.** Observed: FDA issued February 2026 draft guidance for individualized therapies targeting specific genetic conditions, including genome editing and RNA-based therapies ([FDA guidance](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/considerations-use-plausible-mechanism-framework-develop-individualized-therapies-target-specific), [FDA press announcement](https://www.fda.gov/news-events/press-announcements/fda-launches-framework-accelerating-development-individualized-therapies-ultra-rare-diseases)). Observed: Aurora Therapeutics launched in January 2026 around personalized gene editing for rare diseases ([BioSpace](https://www.biospace.com/press-releases/aurora-therapeutics-launches-to-realize-potential-of-personalized-gene-editing-for-millions-of-patients-with-rare-diseases)). Speculation: by 2028, design tools improve faster than regulatory operations capacity, making packet assembly the bottleneck.

**VC / funding / market signal.** YC explicitly asks for AI personalized medicine and notes falling diagnostic and n-of-1 therapy costs ([YC RFS](https://www.ycombinator.com/rfs?year=2025)); Bessemer-backed Converge Bio raised a $25M Series A for generative AI in molecular workflows ([TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/01/13/ai-drug-discovery-startup-converge-bio-pulls-in-25m-from-bessemer-and-execs-from-meta-openai-and-wiz/)).

**How it makes money.** High-ticket per-patient or per-program regulatory operations fee; expand into foundation/payer evidence and manufacturing partner coordination. Service-as-software.

**Strongest counterargument.** The patient count may be too small, and liability is severe. Large CROs, Benchling/Veeva, or specialized regulatory consultancies may dominate once the path is clearer.

**Fit note.** Stretch: high science/regulatory domain depth required.

**Scores.** unsolvedness 5 - the exact workflow is new; timing 4 - FDA signal is fresh but clinical adoption is slow; funding momentum 4 - precision medicine and AI bio are hot; monetization clarity 4 - high ACV but complex payer/foundation funding; market budget 3 - rare-disease budgets are uneven; defensibility-vs-foundation-labs 5 - operational/regulatory execution is not a model-lab feature.

### 5. Industrial EHS incident-to-corrective-action packet service

**Problem.** EHS leaders in manufacturing, energy, construction, and logistics need to turn sensor/video/maintenance records and witness narratives into defensible incident root-cause, corrective-action, training, and regulator/insurer packets after every serious near miss or injury, but today the work is manual, slow, and inconsistent.

**Industry & who pays.** Industrial operations / EHS; VP EHS, plant manager, risk manager, insurer loss-control lead.

**Why unsolved today.** EHS platforms track forms and tasks; they do not reliably reconstruct cross-system causes from CCTV, access logs, maintenance events, shift notes, PPE data, and contractor records. Workarounds are consultant investigations and manual document assembly.

**Why 2028-2029.** Observed: Cisco's 2026 Industrial AI report says critical infrastructure sectors are accelerating AI deployments but readiness gaps and cybersecurity determine scale ([Cisco](https://newsroom.cisco.com/content/r/newsroom/en/us/a/y2026/m03/state-of-industrial-ai-report-2026.html)). Observed: NSC/Wolters Kluwer Enablon reported rapid AI adoption among EHS professionals and the need for guardrails ([NSC](https://www.nsc.org/newsroom/report-ehs-ai-adoption-rapid-needs-guardrails)). Speculation: as plants add AI copilots, cameras, and predictive maintenance, the bottleneck shifts from data collection to defensible causality and accountability.

**VC / funding / market signal.** YC wants AI-native service companies in compliance and healthcare administration; this is a similar service replacement in industrial risk ([YC RFS](https://www.ycombinator.com/rfs?year=2025)).

**How it makes money.** Per-incident packet fee plus annual retainer for near-miss analysis and corrective-action tracking. Service-as-software.

**Strongest counterargument.** EHS buyers can be conservative, and incumbents like Enablon, VelocityEHS, Intelex, insurers, and industrial consultants can add AI features. The startup needs to own outcome-quality, not forms.

**Fit note.** Moderate stretch: infra/security intuition helps, industrial EHS domain is new.

**Scores.** unsolvedness 4 - EHS software is recordkeeping-heavy, not causality-heavy; timing 4 - industrial AI data streams are arriving; funding momentum 3 - less obvious VC heat; monetization clarity 4 - incident cost and insurance incentives are clear; market budget 4 - safety/risk budgets exist; defensibility-vs-foundation-labs 5 - site-specific records and liability workflow create moat.

### 6. Agent-interface conformance monitor for transactional software

**Problem.** SaaS and commerce companies exposing APIs, MCP servers, agent.json files, CLIs, and docs to AI agents need to know whether real agents can discover, authenticate, quote, buy, cancel, and recover from errors after every release, but current QA is built around human UI tests.

**Industry & who pays.** AI-native internet / devtools; developer experience, platform, growth engineering, API product owner.

**Why unsolved today.** There are emerging standards like agent.json, WebMCP, W2A, llms.txt, and MCP, but little production-grade conformance testing across heterogeneous agents. A site can be "agent-ready" in docs and still fail when ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or a local agent tries to complete a transaction.

**Why 2028-2029.** Observed: YC's Software for Agents RFS says the next trillion users on the internet will be AI agents and that agents need machine-readable interfaces, APIs, MCPs, CLIs, and thorough docs ([YC RFS](https://www.ycombinator.com/rfs?year=2025)). Observed: agent.json and WebMCP-like efforts are already appearing ([agent.json](https://www.agent-json.org/), [Web-MCP](https://web-mcp.net/)). Speculation: by 2028, agent transaction failure rate becomes a revenue metric just like conversion rate.

**VC / funding / market signal.** YC has multiple Spring 2026 companies around agent infrastructure, including Indexable for agent sandboxes and primitive for agent communication ([Indexable](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/indexable), [primitive](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/primitive)).

**How it makes money.** Usage-based monitoring, certification badge, and enterprise QA suite; sell first to API-heavy SaaS, travel, fintech, healthcare portals, and commerce.

**Strongest counterargument.** Standards may consolidate and platforms may provide their own validators. Also, prior "AI crawler/answer readiness" ideas are adjacent, so the wedge must be transaction completion and regression testing, not SEO for agents.

**Fit note.** Strong fit: AI-native internet plus devtools.

**Scores.** unsolvedness 4 - standards exist but production conformance is thin; timing 5 - agent web is forming now; funding momentum 4 - YC explicitly asks for it; monetization clarity 4 - conversion/reliability budgets are clear; market budget 4 - API businesses pay for QA/monitoring; defensibility-vs-foundation-labs 3 - platform validators are a real threat.

### 7. Dynamic-interface safety and support layer

**Problem.** Enterprise SaaS vendors that let customer coding agents customize screens, workflows, and middleware need to prove each customer-specific interface is secure, accessible, supportable, and policy-compliant, or every account becomes an untestable fork.

**Industry & who pays.** Enterprise software / devtools; SaaS CTO, product platform lead, customer engineering, support ops.

**Why unsolved today.** Feature flags and app builders assume controlled customization. AI-generated per-user or per-account interfaces can mutate faster than QA, support docs, SOC2 controls, accessibility reviews, and permission boundaries.

**Why 2028-2029.** Observed: YC's Dynamic Software Interfaces RFS argues coding agents will let users become their own forward-deployed engineers and radically customize software ([YC RFS](https://www.ycombinator.com/rfs?year=2025)). Speculation: by 2028, customer-specific UI/workflow generation will be a differentiator, but enterprises will demand supportability and compliance guarantees.

**VC / funding / market signal.** Sequoia says AI applications become "doers"; YC says AI collapses software production costs 10-100x and enables SaaS challengers. Both imply a wave of rapidly mutable software, not static products ([Sequoia](https://sequoiacap.com/article/2026-this-is-agi/), [YC RFS](https://www.ycombinator.com/rfs?year=2025)).

**How it makes money.** Per-seat or per-customization validation engine; sell to SaaS vendors as the policy/testing layer that allows agent-built customization without breaking support.

**Strongest counterargument.** This may be too early. If dynamic interfaces remain a YC thought experiment rather than mainstream enterprise behavior, there is no urgent budget.

**Fit note.** Strong fit: devtools/cloud plus new AI-native software infrastructure.

**Scores.** unsolvedness 5 - the problem barely exists today; timing 3 - likely 2028+ not 2026; funding momentum 4 - VC thesis explicit; monetization clarity 3 - budget depends on adoption; market budget 4 - enterprise SaaS vendors have platform budgets; defensibility-vs-foundation-labs 4 - product-specific policies and tests matter.

### 8. Closed-loop discovery lab run auditor

**Problem.** Pharma, materials, and biotech teams using AI agents to propose experiments and automated labs to run them need a tamper-resistant record of hypothesis, protocol, instrument state, raw data, negative results, model inputs, and scientist sign-off, or they cannot trust, reproduce, patent, or regulate AI-generated discoveries.

**Industry & who pays.** Life sciences / materials science; R&D operations, lab automation head, IP counsel, quality/regulatory.

**Why unsolved today.** ELNs and LIMS record lab work, but closed-loop AI discovery creates new gaps: model-suggested experiment rationale, autonomous protocol changes, failed-run provenance, IP inventorship evidence, and cross-lab reproducibility.

**Why 2028-2029.** Observed: YC asks for AI-native discovery engines that run design-make-test-analyze loops in drug discovery, materials science, and protein engineering ([YC RFS](https://www.ycombinator.com/rfs?year=2025)). Observed: recent research warns AI research agents can narrow scientific exploration when they generate ideas at scale ([arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.27905)). Speculation: as autonomous labs become more common, trust and reproducibility become procurement requirements.

**VC / funding / market signal.** AI bio funding is active, including Converge Bio's $25M Series A and broader 2026 biotech financings across AI-enabled platforms ([TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/01/13/ai-drug-discovery-startup-converge-bio-pulls-in-25m-from-bessemer-and-execs-from-meta-openai-and-wiz/), [BioBucks](https://www.biobucks.co/biotech-vc-funding-tracker-2026)).

**How it makes money.** Per-seat/per-instrument SaaS plus validation services for regulated labs; expand into patent evidence and reproducibility reports.

**Strongest counterargument.** Benchling, Dotmatics, Sapio, and lab automation vendors can add this. Prior "AI-generated science verification" failed when framed around citation checking; this must stay grounded in lab-run operations and regulated/IP evidence.

**Fit note.** Stretch: strong software opportunity but requires deep lab workflow knowledge.

**Scores.** unsolvedness 4 - ELNs exist, agentic provenance does not; timing 4 - discovery loops are moving from thesis to deployment; funding momentum 5 - AI bio capital is strong; monetization clarity 4 - labs pay for ELN/LIMS/validation; market budget 4 - pharma/materials R&D budgets are large; defensibility-vs-foundation-labs 4 - integration with instruments and quality systems is defensible.

### 9. Legacy-PBX voice-agent retrofit and compliance operations

**Problem.** Enterprises and franchise operators running Avaya, Cisco UCM, Mitel, and other on-prem phone systems want AI voice automation but cannot rip and replace telephony, and they need call-resolution, escalation, consent, recording, and QA evidence across regulated calls.

**Industry & who pays.** Customer operations / telecom / regulated services; contact-center VP, CIO, franchise operations, compliance.

**Why unsolved today.** Most voice-agent startups assume modern CCaaS. Legacy PBX environments have brittle call routing, custom scripts, local compliance rules, and messy integrations. The operational gap is not just connecting the bot; it is safely automating and proving outcomes.

**Why 2028-2029.** Observed: YC Spring 2026 company Callab says 58% of the $400B call-center industry runs on on-prem phone systems and that AI voice automation usually requires ripping out infrastructure ([YC Callab listing](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/industry/artificial-intelligence)). Speculation: by 2028, voice models will be good enough that legacy infrastructure, not model quality, is the blocker.

**VC / funding / market signal.** Greylock's AI portfolio includes essential-service revenue agents and fully autonomous customer-support voice agent direction through Cresta/Glean discussions ([Greylock AI](https://greylock.com/ai/), [Greylock enterprise agents](https://greylock.com/events/the-enterprise-brain-for-ai-agents/)).

**How it makes money.** Implementation plus per-resolved-call fee; include QA, compliance reports, and escalation labor. Service-as-software.

**Strongest counterargument.** This is already a knife fight. Callab and voice-agent vendors are directly attacking it, and CCaaS incumbents can subsidize migration.

**Fit note.** Moderate fit: AI operations and infra are relevant, but contact-center GTM is its own world.

**Scores.** unsolvedness 3 - exact wedge is being attacked; timing 5 - voice models and enterprise demand are here; funding momentum 4 - YC/Greylock signal is strong; monetization clarity 5 - per-call ROI is simple; market budget 5 - contact centers are huge; defensibility-vs-foundation-labs 3 - labs provide models, but legacy integration can still defend.

### 10. AI data-center permitting and community-benefit packet operator

**Problem.** AI compute developers racing to build data centers need to assemble power, water, emissions, noise, grid-impact, tax-incentive, community-benefit, and local-hearing evidence packages across jurisdictions, but delays and opposition can kill timelines worth billions.

**Industry & who pays.** AI infrastructure / energy / real estate; data-center developer, hyperscaler infrastructure team, private-equity infrastructure owner.

**Why unsolved today.** Existing permitting consultants are human-heavy and jurisdiction-specific. AI data centers add unusual complexity: massive load, water/cooling scrutiny, grid reliability concerns, community backlash, and fast-changing energy procurement structures.

**Why 2028-2029.** Observed: Sequoia predicted 2026 would be a year of data-center delays even as AI adoption rises ([Sequoia](https://sequoiacap.com/article/ai-in-2026-the-tale-of-two-ais/)). Observed: OpenAI's Stargate project targets up to $500B of AI infrastructure investment by 2029 ([Stargate overview](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stargate_LLC)); Anthropic's mega-round and compute demand reinforce that buildout pressure ([Axios](https://www.axios.com/2026/05/28/anthropic-ai-fundraising-openai)). Speculation: by 2028, permitting and social license will be as constraining as GPUs.

**VC / funding / market signal.** AI infrastructure capital is enormous, but this is not another interconnection-study idea; it is the permitting/community evidence layer around compute buildouts.

**How it makes money.** Per-site permitting packet and monitoring retainer; sell to developers and infrastructure funds, with jurisdiction templates compounding over time. Service-as-software.

**Strongest counterargument.** Local permitting is relationship-heavy and legal/political. Incumbent engineering, environmental, and land-use firms may retain the prime contractor role, leaving a startup as a subcontracted automation tool.

**Fit note.** Stretch but interesting: infra angle fits; energy/permitting domain is the gap.

**Scores.** unsolvedness 4 - human consultants dominate and AI-specific requirements are changing; timing 5 - compute buildouts are immediate; funding momentum 5 - AI infrastructure capital is historic; monetization clarity 4 - per-site fees are large; market budget 5 - developers have billions at stake; defensibility-vs-foundation-labs 5 - frontier labs will buy this, not build it as core product.

## Shortlist - top 3 by raw opportunity, ignoring fit

1. **AI data-center permitting and community-benefit packet operator** - biggest budget and most direct link to frontier AI bottlenecks.
2. **Synthetic-claim evidence triage for P&C insurers** - clear AI-created problem, measurable ROI, recurring claims volume.
3. **Post-quantum cryptography migration evidence desk** - strong regulatory/security budget with a service-as-software path.

## One pick best-matched to me

**Agent-interface conformance monitor for transactional software.** It is closest to your AI-native internet/devtools interests, does not require a professional license, and should improve as frontier agents get more capable rather than being killed by them. The main pressure test is whether it can avoid becoming a free validator inside OpenAI/Anthropic/Google agent platforms.
