# Idea scout — 2026-06-02 (run e)

*Industry-agnostic hunt. Deliberately steered OUT of the agent-infra / agent-reliability /
agent-eval / agent-guardrail / agentic-commerce-fraud cluster that every prior digest and
every pressure-tested report has crowded into — and that the pressure-test agent has
killed every single time. See "Why this run looks different" below.*

---

## Why this run looks different (lessons from `./reports/`)

Every idea in `report.json`, `report-1.json`, `report-2.json` died at the **hypothesis stage**,
and the kill reasons rhyme:

1. **"No tool exists" was always false.** Agent security, eval-from-traces, runtime budget
   kill-switches, guardrails, observability, root-cause — each had free OSS (Promptfoo, Garak,
   Evidently, Guardrails AI, Langfuse) *and* a bundling incumbent (LangSmith, Datadog, Vanta,
   Palo Alto Prisma AIRS) already shipping the wedge. Commoditized to $0.
2. **The autonomous-agent buyer doesn't exist at scale yet.** Ideas premised on enterprises
   running thousands of *autonomous, write-access, regulated* agent decisions/month collapsed —
   HITL is mandated, deployments are pilots, the ICP is "a slice of a slice of a slice."
3. **Regulatory "why now" kept slipping.** EU AI Act Art. 12/26 → Dec 2027 (Digital Omnibus);
   CMS-0057-F removed the prior-auth plumbing wedge. Deadline-driven urgency is fragile.
4. **Foundation labs / platforms absorb the layer.** "A lab will just do this" was real.
5. **Service-as-software back-office has graveyards** where it was tried badly: Olive AI
   ($850M, prior-auth), Certara divestiture (AI-science verification), Humanloop acqui-hire.

**The structural fix this run applies to every idea:** pick spaces where (a) the buyer pays a
**human service firm TODAY** (budget is proven, not contingent on future autonomous agents),
(b) the moat is something a frontier lab structurally **won't** own — a license, physical
access, a regulated data set, deep vertical integration — and (c) "why now" rests on a **cost
curve crossing**, not a single statute. Diversity requirement honored: 6 ideas, 6 distinct
industries, **none** in the killed cluster.

## Step 2 — Forecast: the AI-shaped world of 2028-2029

**Observed signal driving the forecast.** In H1 2026 the frontier curve bent in three ways that
compound by 2028-2029: million-token context became economically viable, structured outputs hit
production-grade reliability, and "computer use" / long-horizon agent loops became standard
product infrastructure rather than demos (Anthropic Managed Agents + agent teams orchestrating
"tens to hundreds of agents," OpenAI Codex background computer use, Gemini Computer Use). Cost
per token is collapsing — MiMo-V2.5 matches frontier agentic benchmarks at 40-60% fewer tokens;
Blackwell low-precision drove a ~4x cost cut. Capital is validating the direction: OpenAI's
$122B round, Anthropic's $65B, and — most tellingly for *this* digest — **every major VC's 2026
thesis converged on the same sentence: stop selling tools, sell finished work.** YC's Summer
2026 RFS explicitly asks for "AI-native service companies that sell completed work, not tools, in
insurance brokerage, accounting, tax, audit, compliance and healthcare administration," and to
"push AI into the physical world" (agriculture, semiconductors, defense, energy). a16z's bet is
AI delivering $200/hr professional services at mass-market prices.

**What that world looks like.** By 2028-2029, generic cognition is cheap, reliable enough for
bounded tasks, and commoditized across 4-5 labs. The scarce, defensible things are no longer
"can an agent reason" but: **(1) legal authority to act** (who is allowed to press the button —
a licensed broker, a chartered engineer, an insurer of record); **(2) physical or proprietary
access** to the real world and to messy private data; and **(3) accountability when the work is
wrong.** The economic action moves from horizontal agent infra (a knife fight the labs win) to
**vertically-integrated "agent does the whole job" businesses in unglamorous, licensed, or
physically-gated industries** that the labs will never enter because the moat is non-cognitive.

**Derived problem spaces (SPECULATION clearly labeled).** If cognition is cheap and the bottleneck
is authority/access/accountability, then the open problems of 2028-2029 are: industries where a
*human service firm* is the current product, the work is routine-but-judgment-laden, and a
software-native firm that holds the license / owns the data / controls the physical loop can
deliver the outcome at 10-50x lower cost. The bet (SPECULATION) is that **the winners are
operating companies, not API vendors** — they capture margin a lab can't, precisely because the
hard part isn't the model. Below are six, in six industries, each chosen because the buyer and
budget are **observable today** and the defensibility is structural.

## 1. AI-native *licensed* customs brokerage (service-as-software where the license is the moat)

- **Problem.** A mid-market importer (≈$20M-$500M goods/yr) files hundreds to thousands of customs
  entries a year; each requires HTS classification, valuation, duty/PGA determination, and an entry
  that — by law — only a *licensed US customs broker* can file. Classification errors mean
  penalties, seizures, and overpaid duty; brokers are a shrinking, aging, license-gated workforce
  and turnaround is slow and expensive, made worse by 2026's tariff volatility.
- **Industry & who pays.** Global trade / logistics. Budget holder: importers' trade-compliance and
  finance teams, who today pay per-entry fees to brokerage firms (Livingston, C.H. Robinson, thousands
  of regional brokers).
- **Why unsolved today.** AI tools exist (TariffLens, Gaia Dynamics, GingerControl) but they're
  *assistive* — CBP's "nexus to entry" test and HQ H350722 (Jan 16 2026) confirm an AI tool that
  directs classification beyond 6 digits is "conducting customs business," and **the entry button
  must legally be pressed by a licensed US broker.** So tools sell software to incumbent brokers;
  nobody has built the *vertically-integrated brokerage that holds the license and runs the agent
  internally*, delivering the entry as a finished outcome.
- **Why 2028-2029.** SIGNAL: first-pass AI HTS accuracy is already 85-95% with confidence scoring
  and reasoning chains. SPECULATION: by 2028 the licensed-broker-in-the-loop reviews exceptions only,
  collapsing cost-per-entry; the firm that holds the license + the model + the importer relationship
  out-economics every assistive-tool vendor selling into legacy brokers.
- **VC / funding / market signal.** YC Summer 2026 RFS names "AI-native service companies that sell
  completed work… in compliance"; multiple AI-HTS tools funded/shipping in 2026 (validation the
  problem is real). No sourced player has taken the "become the licensed brokerage" path.
- **How it makes money.** Per-entry / per-shipment fee (service-as-software), identical billing to
  the firm being displaced, at structurally lower cost; land via tariff-pain outbound to importers.
- **Strongest counterargument.** Becoming a licensed brokerage is a regulated, capital- and
  ops-heavy slog (you must employ licensed brokers, carry a customs bond, pass CBP scrutiny) — this
  is an *operating company*, not a software company, with software-company-low margins until scale.
  The incumbents (C.H. Robinson with 30+ freight agents already) can bolt the same AI onto their
  existing license and distribution faster than a startup can earn a license.
- **Fit note.** Stretch. Regulated-trade ops is far from Aditya's security/agent wedge; the moat is a
  license, not reliability engineering. The *agent-reliability* core fits, the domain does not.
- **Scores.** Unsolvedness 4 (assistive tools only; integrated brokerage open) · Timing 4 (tariff
  chaos + accuracy crossing) · Funding momentum 3 (tools funded, integrated play unfunded) ·
  Monetization clarity 5 (proven per-entry fee) · Market budget 4 (large, recurring) ·
  Defensibility-vs-labs 5 (a lab will never hold a customs license).

## 2. Commercial-property loss-control / risk-engineering surveys as software (insurance)

- **Problem.** A commercial P&C insurer or broker must physically inspect insured buildings
  (sprinklers, roof, electrical, occupancy, COPE data) and produce a risk-engineering report that
  drives pricing and binding. A loss-control engineer drives to a site, walks it, and writes the
  report — days of skilled labor per property, against a **shrinking, aging risk-engineer workforce**,
  so most mid-market risks are surveyed rarely or never and priced on stale/guessed data.
- **Industry & who pays.** Commercial insurance. Budget holder: carrier underwriting + loss-control
  departments and MGAs, who fund internal engineers and outside survey firms today.
- **Why unsolved today.** Insurers are explicitly risk-averse and slow on AI, but **insurance AI spend
  is growing 25%+ in 2026** — budget without product. The work is gated by physical access and
  proprietary COPE/claims data, not cognition, so horizontal AI hasn't touched it; the workaround is
  "inspect a fraction, model the rest."
- **Why 2028-2029.** SIGNAL: multimodal models now read site imagery/video reliably; drones and phone
  capture are cheap. SPECULATION: by 2028 a survey is captured by a low-cost field tech (or drone) and
  the *engineering judgment + report* is produced by an agent, with a licensed engineer signing
  exceptions — 10x more properties surveyed at a fraction of cost, feeding better pricing.
- **VC / funding / market signal.** Insurance named one of the lowest-AI-adoption-yet-highest-budget
  sectors with 25%+ spend growth (InsuranceNewsNet / Cognizant 2026); YC RFS names insurance
  service-as-software. (SIGNAL: budget + thesis; SPECULATION: this specific wedge is under-built.)
- **How it makes money.** Per-survey fee to carriers/MGAs (service-as-software replacing survey
  firms), or a data subscription on the resulting risk-engineering dataset.
- **Strongest counterargument.** Carriers buy *evidence and defensibility*, not "the report" — and
  they distrust ungoverned AI on data they can't put in models. Sales cycles into carriers are
  brutally long (12-36 months), and the physical-capture leg makes this part field-ops company, not
  pure software. Incumbent loss-control/insurtech (e.g., the established survey networks, CoreLogic-style
  data shops) can bundle.
- **Fit note.** Stretch. Insurance buyer credibility and physical field-ops are outside Aditya's edge;
  the agent-reliability-on-judgment-tasks core is a fit, the go-to-market is not.
- **Scores.** Unsolvedness 4 · Timing 3 (budget rising, but carrier inertia) · Funding momentum 2
  (quiet vs the hype cluster — good or bad) · Monetization clarity 4 · Market budget 5 (huge P&C
  spend) · Defensibility-vs-labs 4 (physical access + proprietary COPE data).

## 3. Veterinary-group back office & revenue operations as software (animal health)

- **Problem.** A multi-site veterinary group (independent chains + PE-consolidated platforms) runs on
  fragmented legacy PIMS (Avimark, Cornerstone) with manual scheduling, invoicing, client comms,
  inventory, and collections across clinics. Under PE debt pressure and a severe labor shortage,
  back-office and front-desk work is the bottleneck — overworked staff, missed billing, churned
  clients — and there's no Epic-equivalent backbone to standardize it.
- **Industry & who pays.** Veterinary / animal health. Budget holder: vet-group operators and PE
  platforms (AmeriVet, etc.); cash-pay, so **no insurance-plumbing trap** that killed human-health RCM.
- **Why unsolved today.** >30% of GP practices and ~75% of specialty/ER are now corporately owned, but
  the PIMS market is split between legacy server systems and thin cloud tools (PIMS market $1.52B→$4.57B,
  12.7% CAGR). The pain is operational glue across sites and legacy systems — exactly the messy
  integration work labs won't do. Workaround: hire more (scarce, expensive) staff.
- **Why 2028-2029.** SIGNAL: voice + computer-use agents now handle phones, scheduling, and legacy-UI
  data entry. SPECULATION: by 2028 a vet group runs front-desk + RCM + inventory as an agent layer over
  whatever PIMS each clinic has, billed per clinic/per outcome — the operational standardization PE
  roll-ups desperately need to hit margin targets.
- **VC / funding / market signal.** PE consolidation + labor bottleneck documented (Private Equity Vet;
  TransitionsElite 2026 outlook); PIMS market CAGR 12.7%. (SIGNAL: consolidation/labor; SPECULATION:
  agent back-office layer is the unbuilt wedge.)
- **How it makes money.** Per-clinic SaaS + per-outcome (collections recovered, calls handled);
  land one PE platform, expand across its portfolio (built-in distribution).
- **Strongest counterargument.** This is a crowded vertical-SaaS space (the six modern cloud PIMS are
  all bolting on AI) and PE buyers squeeze vendor pricing hard; "back office for vets" may be a feature
  the incumbent PIMS ships, not a standalone company. Healthcare-adjacent ops with low willingness to pay
  per clinic could cap ACV below venture scale.
- **Fit note.** Stretch. No animal-health domain edge or network; pure vertical-SaaS execution, far
  from the reliability/security wedge.
- **Scores.** Unsolvedness 3 (PIMS incumbents circling) · Timing 4 (PE pressure + labor crisis now) ·
  Funding momentum 2 · Monetization clarity 4 · Market budget 3 (mid-size, growing) ·
  Defensibility-vs-labs 4 (legacy integration + portfolio lock-in).

## 4. Independent retrofit autonomy for the existing farm sprayer fleet (agriculture)

- **Problem.** Row-crop and especially specialty-crop growers blanket-spray entire fields because
  per-plant targeting is locked inside new OEM equipment (John Deere See & Spray) most can't afford.
  Chemical costs rise as pests adapt, margins fall, and the ~$1M+ machines the farmer already owns
  stay "dumb." The grower wants the chemical-reduction outcome without buying a new fleet.
- **Industry & who pays.** Agriculture / ag-inputs. Budget holder: growers and ag retailers/co-ops,
  who pay per acre for inputs and custom application today.
- **Why unsolved today.** YC's Summer 2026 RFS says it plainly: "AI can now identify individual weeds
  and pests in real time, sensors and cameras are cheap enough to deploy at scale, robotics can treat
  one plant." The capability exists; what's missing is an **OEM-independent retrofit** (vision + control
  kit + agronomic model) for the installed base, especially specialty crops Deere underserves. Workaround:
  buy new OEM gear or keep blanket-spraying.
- **Why 2028-2029.** SIGNAL: cheap edge cameras + on-device multimodal + falling inference cost.
  SPECULATION: by 2028 a bolt-on kit + per-acre agronomy service pays for itself in one season of chemical
  savings, sold as outcome (% input reduction guaranteed).
- **VC / funding / market signal.** YC Summer 2026 RFS explicitly requests this; broad "AI into the
  physical world" convergence across YC/a16z/Sequoia. (SIGNAL: explicit RFS; SPECULATION: independent
  retrofit model unproven at scale.)
- **How it makes money.** Hardware-light kit + per-acre recurring agronomy/outcome fee; channel via ag
  retailers who already sell inputs.
- **Strongest counterargument.** This is a hardware + field-reliability + seasonal-sales-cycle business
  with thin software margins and a brutal go-to-market (one selling season a year, conservative buyers);
  Deere and the input majors (Bayer, Corteva) can crush or acquire, and "retrofit on a 20-year-old machine
  in a dusty field" reliability is genuinely hard. Capital intensity is high for a solo founder.
- **Fit note.** Hard stretch. Hardware + agronomy is the opposite of Aditya's software/agent/security
  profile; capital and field ops make it a poor solo-founder fit despite the strong market.
- **Scores.** Unsolvedness 4 (OEM-locked; retrofit open) · Timing 4 (cost curves crossed) · Funding
  momentum 4 (top of every RFS) · Monetization clarity 3 (per-acre proven, but seasonal) · Market budget
  4 (ag-inputs are enormous) · Defensibility-vs-labs 5 (physical/agronomic; no lab competes).

## 5. The "transaction-truth + post-purchase resolution" backend for agent-driven commerce (AI-native internet)

- **Problem.** As AI agents move from *recommending* to *buying* (ChatGPT Instant Checkout, Google UCP
  Native Checkout, Visa Intelligent Commerce), a new failure class appears: agents transact on **stale or
  wrong product truth** — price, real-time stock, variant/attributes, shipping eligibility — causing failed
  orders, wrong purchases, and a flood of returns/disputes no human initiated. When an agent buys the wrong
  thing for a customer, *who reconciles it, and against what source of truth?* Merchants have no backend
  built for machine-speed, machine-initiated post-purchase resolution.
- **Industry & who pays.** E-commerce / payments infrastructure. Budget holder: mid-market merchants and
  the platforms/PSPs whose checkout the agents hit.
- **Why unsolved today.** 2026's protocol race (ACP, UCP, Mastercard Agent Suite) standardizes *checkout
  and KYA*, and AEO (Profound, now a $1B unicorn + 9 rivals) owns *discoverability* — but the **truth/feed
  accuracy + automated dispute/return resolution layer for agent orders** is unclaimed. With agents
  projected at 10-20% of e-commerce ($190-385B, Morgan Stanley AlphaWise), the workaround (human CS + manual
  feeds) breaks. NOTE: this is deliberately *not* the agentic-commerce *fraud/consent* idea already covered
  in prior digests.
- **Why 2028-2029.** SIGNAL: agent checkout is live and protocol-backed now. SPECULATION: by 2028 agent
  order volume forces a real-time merchant truth-layer + autonomous resolution rail, or chargebacks and
  failed orders make merchants un-buyable by agents.
- **VC / funding / market signal.** ACP/UCP live (OpenAI+Stripe, Google); Profound $96M Series C at $1B
  (Feb 2026) proves brands will pay to be agent-ready; Mastercard/Visa agent rails shipping. (SIGNAL: heavy;
  SPECULATION: the resolution/truth layer specifically is early.)
- **How it makes money.** Per-transaction or per-resolution fee + SaaS to merchants; PSP/platform
  partnerships for distribution.
- **Strongest counterargument.** This is exactly the layer the **platforms absorb**: Stripe/Shopify/Google
  own checkout and the merchant relationship and will extend into truth + resolution natively (Shopify feeds,
  Stripe disputes) — a thin third-party layer gets squeezed, the classic "a platform will just do this" kill.
  And until agent order volume is actually large, the pain is speculative, not bleeding.
- **Fit note.** Plausible fit. This is "AI-native internet" (a stated interest) and the hard part is
  *reliable autonomous reconciliation at machine speed* — squarely Aditya's long-horizon-agent edge — but he
  lacks commerce/payments distribution. Buildable; GTM is the gap.
- **Scores.** Unsolvedness 4 (truth/resolution layer open) · Timing 3 (early — volume not there yet) ·
  Funding momentum 4 (commerce-agent capital is pouring in) · Monetization clarity 4 (per-txn proven shape)
  · Market budget 4 · Defensibility-vs-labs 3 (labs won't, but *platforms* might).

## 6. Utility-side DER interconnection study automation (energy / utilities)

- **Problem.** Distribution utilities and grid operators are buried under a flood of interconnection
  requests — rooftop/community solar, batteries, EV depots, and data-center loads — each requiring an
  impact/hosting-capacity study by scarce planning engineers. Queues stretch **5+ years**; transmission
  permitting 7-10 years; **>$1.5T of infrastructure capital is stuck in the pipeline.** The bottleneck is
  the *utility's own* study throughput, not the developer's paperwork.
- **Industry & who pays.** Energy / electric utilities. Budget holder: utility planning/interconnection
  departments and the engineering consultancies they outsource studies to — utilities are spending $1.4T
  capex (+27% YoY) and the DOE is explicitly funding AI to do interconnection "20-100x faster."
- **Why unsolved today.** Energy is a low-AI-adoption, legacy, data-migration-bound sector; studies are
  done by hand in specialized tools by a thin engineering bench. The workaround is "hire consultants, wait
  years." NOTE: distinct from the *developer/project-side* "permitting & queue" idea in run-b — this targets
  the **utility engineering department** drowning in study volume.
- **Why 2028-2029.** SIGNAL: DOE's 26 challenges name AI-for-interconnection at 20-100x speed; grid
  connectivity called *the* strategic AI bottleneck (WEF 2026). SPECULATION: by 2028 a study that took a
  quarter is produced in days by an agent over the utility's grid model, with an engineer of record signing
  off — service-as-software for the planning desk.
- **VC / funding / market signal.** $1.4T utility capex (+27%), $1.5T capital stuck, DOE RFIs and grid
  investment ($1.9B), Morgan Stanley/WEF/IEA all flagging the bottleneck. (SIGNAL: very strong on budget &
  urgency; SPECULATION: utility-side study-automation product under-built.)
- **How it makes money.** Per-study / per-MW fee to utilities + consultancies, or enterprise license to
  utility planning depts; land via a consultancy partner that already holds the relationships.
- **Strongest counterargument.** Utilities are the slowest enterprise buyers on earth (multi-year
  procurement, regulated, risk-averse), the grid models and study methods are utility-specific (no clean
  product across utilities), and the engineer-of-record liability means a human still owns every study —
  capping the labor savings. Specialized incumbents (PSS®E/Siemens, and grid-resilience players like
  Overstory who already serve 6 of the 10 largest utilities) can extend into it.
- **Fit note.** Stretch. Deep grid-engineering domain and utility sales are far from Aditya's wedge,
  though the long-horizon-agent-over-a-real-model core fits.
- **Scores.** Unsolvedness 4 (utility-side study automation open) · Timing 4 (DOE push + queue crisis) ·
  Funding momentum 3 (energy-AI capital real but study-automation quiet) · Monetization clarity 4 ·
  Market budget 5 ($1.4T capex) · Defensibility-vs-labs 4 (utility-specific models + engineer-of-record).

## Shortlist — top 2 by raw opportunity (ignoring fit)

1. **#1 AI-native licensed customs brokerage.** Best survivor of the report-kill patterns: the buyer pays a
   human firm *today*, "why now" is a cost-curve crossing (85-95% HTS accuracy) rather than a slipping
   statute, and the **license is a moat a frontier lab structurally cannot cross** — the entry button must be
   pressed by a licensed broker. Open field (everyone else is selling tools to incumbent brokers). Tariff
   chaos is a live, non-AI tailwind.
2. **#6 Utility-side DER interconnection study automation.** Largest budget on the list ($1.4T utility capex,
   $1.5T stuck), explicit DOE pull for AI at 20-100x, and a moat (utility-specific grid models +
   engineer-of-record) labs won't touch. Downside is glacial procurement — but the budget and urgency are the
   most concrete, sourced signals in this digest.

## One pick best-matched to me (profile as tiebreaker only)

**#5 — the transaction-truth + post-purchase resolution backend for agent-driven commerce.** It's the only
idea here that lands on a *stated interest* ("AI-native internet") **and** leans on the exact edge — reliable
**autonomous reconciliation at machine speed**, long-horizon agent execution, schema/structured-output
correctness — that is Aditya's sharpest, hardest-to-copy skill. It's B2B, OSS-to-enterprise-able, and can show
real usage fast. The honest caveat: it has the weakest defensibility on the list (platforms, not labs, are the
threat) and Aditya has no commerce/payments distribution — so it's the best *fit*, not the best *opportunity*.
Everything else is a clear stretch on domain and network, even where the agent-reliability core would transfer.
