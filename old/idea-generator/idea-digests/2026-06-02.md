# Idea scout — 2026-06-02

Five emerging, currently under-solved problem spaces plausibly hot/fundable/monetizable
in 2028–2029, filtered through the founder profile (long-horizon agent reliability ·
agent evals/observability · offensive security; B2B, OSS→enterprise, technical buyer,
~1yr to revenue). All five are deliberately distinct from the 2026-06-01 runs (which
covered: MCP supply-chain, model-upgrade regression gating, budget circuit-breakers,
browser injection defense, agent insurer scoring, EU AI Act evidence layer, adversarial
sim envs, voice guardrails, legacy-migration equivalence, merchant agentic-commerce fraud,
release-gating cert, continuous red-teaming, root-cause diagnosis, A2A identity, outcome-
priced security services).

**Read on the landscape:** the reliability *infrastructure* layer (durable execution,
observability) is consolidating into large, well-funded players — competing head-on there
is a losing move for a solo founder. The open lanes are the *correctness, integrity, and
reversibility* gaps these players leave behind, plus security-specific verticals where
buyer credibility is the moat. The five below target those gaps.

## 1. Auto-mined regression & eval suites from production agent traces (the "eval-data bottleneck")

**Problem.** A team running a production agent ships a prompt/model/tool change weekly and
cannot answer "did this make the agent worse on the cases that actually occur?" because they
have no representative eval set — eval suites today are hand-built, tiny, and stale the day
they're written. The agent that broke is found by a customer, not a test. This recurs on
every change, per team, and the cost is silent quality regression in high-stakes flows.

**Why unsolved today.** Observability players (InsightFinder, Datadog, Langfuse) capture
traces and *diagnose* incidents after the fact; Temporal captures activity logs for
*recovery*. Neither turns the firehose of real traces into a labeled, deduplicated, drift-
weighted regression suite with expected outcomes. The hard part is auto-labeling "what
*should* have happened" without a human on every trace — exactly the eval-engineering problem
most teams punt on. Current workaround: a junior eng hand-curates ~50 golden cases and prays.

**Why 2028–2029.** *Signal:* observability rounds (InsightFinder $15M) and Temporal's $5B
validate that production agent data is the new center of gravity. *Speculation:* by 2028,
once agents are in regulated revenue paths, "prove this change didn't regress" becomes a
release requirement, and the team that owns the eval-set-generation layer sits upstream of
both CI and compliance. What must be true: trace volume high enough to mine, and auto-labeling
good enough to trust (the bet).

**Funding / market signal.** Adjacent observability/eval funding is real and sourced
(InsightFinder $15M Series B, Yu Galaxy, Apr 2026; Temporal $300M, a16z, Feb 2026). Eval-data
*generation* specifically is not yet a named funded category — open lane, not saturation.

**How it makes money.** OSS trace-to-eval harness (promptfoo-adjacent, his actual toolchain)
→ paid cloud for continuous mining, drift weighting, and CI gating; usage/seat hybrid. Sales
motion: founder-led to AI-platform teams at YC/AI-agent companies he can reach.

**My edge.** This is his sharpest non-security edge: he has built agent evals/benchmarking
(promptfoo), observability (Langfuse), and self-correcting schema SDKs in production. He knows
where eval sets lie because he's debugged 50+hr runs.

**Strongest counterargument.** The incumbents (InsightFinder, Datadog, LangSmith) are one
feature away from "turn traces into evals," have the data already, and distribution. A
standalone eval-data tool risks being a feature, not a company, unless auto-labeling is a
genuine technical moat — and if LLM-as-judge labeling is "good enough," everyone ships it.

**Scores.** Unsolvedness 4 (real gap, but adjacent to crowded observability) · Timing 3
(early; becomes a requirement later, not yet) · Funding momentum 4 (category hot, sub-niche
unfunded) · Monetization clarity 3 (risks feature-not-company) · Founder fit 5 (dead-center
evals edge) · Defensibility 2 (incumbents can absorb).

## 2. Memory-integrity & poisoning defense for long-horizon agents

**Problem.** An agent that runs for hours/days accumulates persistent memory (vector stores,
scratchpads, summaries it writes back). A single poisoned or hallucinated entry persists and
compounds across the whole run and future runs — corrupting decisions long after the bad input,
with no trace pointing back to the cause. Severe for any agent with real authority (security,
fintech, ops); happens silently and is near-impossible to debug after the fact.

**Why unsolved today.** Observability shows *what* the agent did, not that its *memory* was
trustworthy. Injection defenses target the input turn, not the accumulated state. There is no
standard for memory provenance, integrity checks, or "quarantine + rollback of poisoned
memory." Workaround: teams wipe memory between runs (defeating the point) or eyeball it.

**Why 2028–2029.** *Signal:* the field is openly moving to longer-horizon, memory-heavy
agents (Temporal's whole pitch is multi-hour stateful runs; NeoCognition's $40M seed is for
agents that "learn like humans," i.e. persistent memory). *Speculation:* as memory horizons
grow, memory poisoning becomes the dominant silent-failure and attack class — the bet is that
this becomes a named threat category with budget by 2028, the way prompt injection did.

**Funding / market signal.** No clean standalone funding round found for memory-integrity
specifically (honest: early/unproven). Indirect: durable-stateful-agent investment (Temporal
$300M) and learning-agent investment (NeoCognition $40M seed, TechCrunch 2026-04-21) imply the
memory surface is growing fast.

**How it makes money.** OSS memory-integrity middleware (provenance tagging, integrity
attestations, poison detection, quarantine/rollback) → enterprise edition with audit + SOC2-
grade evidence; usage-based. Founder-led to agent builders, then security buyers.

**My edge.** Double-stacked: long-horizon unsupervised execution (he's lived the compounding-
error failure mode) *and* offensive security (he can both poison and defend memory, building
the attacker and defender as one product — like Pentest Copilot's dual edition).

**Strongest counterargument.** Timing may be too early — most production agents in 2026 are
short-horizon and stateless-by-design precisely to avoid this, so the addressable market may
not materialize until well into the 2028 window, risking a long pre-revenue arc that violates
his "real usage within a year" constraint.

**Scores.** Unsolvedness 5 (genuinely open) · Timing 2 (likely early for revenue) · Funding
momentum 2 (no direct rounds yet) · Monetization clarity 3 · Founder fit 5 (perfect edge-stack)
· Defensibility 4 (attacker+defender know-how is rare).

## 3. Safe-interruption + semantic rollback ("undo / clean kill-switch") for agents with real-world side effects

**Problem.** When a long-running agent must be halted mid-task — it's looping, going rogue, or
a human spots a mistake — there is no way to stop it *and* cleanly reverse the partial real-world
side effects it already committed (half-sent payments, partial DB writes, emails dispatched,
tickets opened). Operators face a binary: let it finish (dangerous) or kill it and leave the
external world in a corrupt, half-finished state. Per-incident, severe, and the failure is
financial/operational.

**Why unsolved today.** Temporal gives durable execution and *resume* (pick up where you left
off) — the opposite of what's needed here, which is *trustworthy reversal*. Budget circuit-
breakers (covered 2026-06-01) just stop spend; they don't undo effects. No one provides a
side-effect ledger with compensating-action orchestration scoped to agents. Workaround: pray,
or build bespoke sagas per workflow.

**Why 2028–2029.** *Signal:* agents are being given real tool authority now (Blumira lets an
agent decide which alerts a human sees; agentic commerce/payments emerging). *Speculation:* the
first few public "agent committed an irreversible action" disasters (the deleted-prod-DB class
already seen in 2025) push "provable safe interrupt + rollback" into procurement checklists by
2028. Bet: regulators/insurers start demanding a demonstrable kill-and-revert capability.

**Funding / market signal.** No direct funded peer found (early). Indirectly the inverse is
heavily funded — Temporal's $300M for durable *resume* validates that state/side-effect
management is a billion-dollar problem; rollback is the unbuilt other half.

**How it makes money.** OSS side-effect ledger + compensating-action SDK (integrates with tool
registries — his core competence) → enterprise control plane with audit; usage-based per
guarded action. Founder-led to fintech/ops agent teams (his fintech buyer credibility helps).

**My edge.** Core agent-loop, tool-registry, and distributed-execution depth — he's built the
exact machinery (tool calls, error recovery, long-horizon control) that a rollback layer must
hook into. Few people understand both the agent loop and transactional reversal.

**Strongest counterargument.** This may belong inside the agent framework or the durable-
execution platform (Temporal, LangGraph) rather than as a third-party layer — incumbents own
the execution substrate where rollback most naturally lives, and may bundle it, leaving a
standalone tool stranded.

**Scores.** Unsolvedness 5 · Timing 3 (real but a notch early) · Funding momentum 3 (inverse
is funded; this niche isn't) · Monetization clarity 4 (clear per-action pricing) · Founder fit
5 · Defensibility 3 (platform-bundling risk).

## 4. Correctness assurance for autonomous defensive-security agents (the "did the SOC agent miss a real attack?" layer)

**Problem.** Vendors now ship agentic SOCs that decide which alerts a human ever sees (Blumira
Kindling: 30–50× alert suppression, "three-judge AI consensus"). The buyer's unanswerable
question: *what did it silently suppress that was a real breach?* A false negative here is an
undetected intrusion, and there is no independent, continuous way to measure an autonomous
defensive agent's true-positive recall in the buyer's own environment. Per-deployment, per-
model-update, existential when wrong.

**Why unsolved today.** The agentic-SOC vendors grade themselves (Blumira's own 98.5% figure,
validated on their own incidents). No neutral layer continuously injects known-malicious
signal and measures whether the defensive agent catches it — an *eval/red-team harness aimed
at defensive agents*, distinct from attacking the customer's app (covered 2026-06-01).
Workaround: trust the vendor's marketing number.

**Why 2028–2029.** *Signal:* agentic SIEM/SOC is shipping to SMB/MSP *now* (Blumira, May 2026;
broader agentic-security wave — JupiterOne, Oracle advisory). *Speculation:* as these tools
move up-market and suppress more, a 2027-class "the AI SOC missed the breach" incident makes
independent assurance a buying requirement; MSPs especially need it to defend their own
liability to clients. Bet: buyers stop accepting vendor-graded accuracy.

**Funding / market signal.** Agentic-SOC adoption is concrete (Blumira Kindling launch,
SiliconANGLE 2026-05-13; Blumira ~$28M raised total). The *assurance/verification* counter-
layer is unfunded — classic picks-and-shovels open lane beside a hot market.

**How it makes money.** Continuous purple-team harness sold to (a) MSPs/enterprises buying
agentic SOCs and (b) the SOC vendors themselves as a "verified-by" badge; subscription +
usage. Founder-led to security buyers — his strongest distribution.

**My edge.** All three advantages at once: offensive security (he builds the attack signal),
agent evals (he measures recall rigorously), and direct security-buyer credibility from BugBase
(HDFC, PhonePe, Govt of India). This is the most distribution-aligned idea on the board.

**Strongest counterargument.** The market may be small and squeezed: MSPs are price-sensitive,
the agentic-SOC vendors will resist external grading (and may self-certify "good enough"), and
incumbent pentest/BAS players (which BugBase itself competes near) can extend into "test your
defensive agent" — so he'd be fighting both the vendors and adjacent incumbents for a thin wedge.

**Scores.** Unsolvedness 4 · Timing 4 (market live now) · Funding momentum 3 (market hot,
counter-layer unfunded) · Monetization clarity 3 (price-sensitive buyers) · Founder fit 5
(triple edge-stack + distribution) · Defensibility 3 (vendor resistance, incumbent reach).

## 5. Buyer-side agent assurance / "agent due-diligence" reports for procured third-party agents

**Problem.** An enterprise about to deploy a *third-party* agent (a vendor's SOC agent, a SaaS
copilot with tool access, an outsourced ops agent) has no standardized way to assess its
reliability, attack surface, data handling, and failure behavior before granting it privileges.
Procurement runs a static security questionnaire designed for SaaS, which says nothing about an
agent's runtime behavior. Every new agent vendor and every renewal needs this, and the blast
radius is the agent's full delegated authority inside the buyer's systems.

**Why unsolved today.** Vendor-side trust signals exist (DigiCert pushing identity/crypto
governance; SOC2) but none probe *agent behavior*. There is no "agent due-diligence report" —
an automated battery that exercises a candidate agent (injection susceptibility, tool-misuse,
drift under load, refusal-bypass) and produces a standardized risk grade a buyer can act on.
Workaround: a spreadsheet questionnaire and a vibe check.

**Why 2028–2029.** *Signal:* the trust/governance theme is loud and incumbent-active now
(DigiCert "AI trust crisis," Oracle pre-emptive advisory, Celonis buying Ikigai for "operational
context"). *Speculation:* as enterprises buy dozens of third-party agents, a standardized
procurement-gate report becomes the norm (the way SOC2 became table stakes), and whoever defines
the rubric early owns the standard. Bet: a recognized assurance standard emerges in the window.

**Funding / market signal.** Trust/governance investment is real but vendor-side and incumbent-
led (DigiCert, JupiterOne AI-sprawl tooling, SiliconANGLE May 2026). A *buyer-side behavioral*
assurance report is not a named funded category — open, but unproven demand.

**How it makes money.** Per-report / subscription to enterprise buyers and procurement teams;
optional "trust badge" upsell to vendors who pass (two-sided). Founder-led to security/procurement
buyers; his BugBase credibility opens the door.

**My edge.** Offensive security + agent internals: he can design the behavioral probe battery
(it's Pentest Copilot pointed at an agent instead of an app) and has the buyer relationships to
land the rubric. Reuses his "trust through transparency / OSS" instinct to seed an open standard.

**Strongest counterargument.** Selling to procurement is slow, cyclical, and unglamorous —
violates his "real usage fast" preference — and a report-based business is hard to make venture-
scale unless it becomes the *de facto standard*, which standards-bodies, big-4 auditors, or the
cloud platforms are better positioned to impose than a solo founder.

**Scores.** Unsolvedness 4 · Timing 3 (norm not yet formed) · Funding momentum 2 (buyer-side
unfunded) · Monetization clarity 3 (report businesses scale hard) · Founder fit 4 (security edge
strong; procurement motion off-profile) · Defensibility 3 (standard-capture risk).
