## Step 2 forecast - the AI-shaped world of 2028-2029

**Observed signal.** Frontier labs are moving from chat interfaces to delegated work: OpenAI describes Codex as a cloud software-engineering agent that can work on tasks in parallel, and its Responses API/Agents SDK are explicitly for tool-using agents ([OpenAI Codex](https://openai.com/index/introducing-codex/), [OpenAI agents tools](https://openai.com/index/new-tools-for-building-agents/)). Anthropic has pushed Claude Code and high-end coding/reasoning models deeper into enterprise developer workflows ([Anthropic Claude 4](https://www.anthropic.com/news/claude-4), [Claude Code](https://www.anthropic.com/claude-code)). YC's current RFS list is unusually direct about AI-native agencies, AI for government, AI guidance for physical work, stablecoin financial services, modern metal mills, infrastructure for fraud hunters, and AI-native hedge funds ([YC RFS](https://www.ycombinator.com/rfs)).

**Observed signal.** Capital is already validating vertical work-execution markets: Abridge raised a $300M Series E for clinical AI documentation ([Abridge](https://www.abridge.com/press/abridge-raises-300m-series-e)), OpenEvidence raised a large Series B in medical AI ([OpenEvidence](https://www.openevidence.com/news)), Harvey raised major legal-AI capital ([Harvey](https://www.harvey.ai/blog/harvey-raises-300m-series-e)), ORO Labs raised $100M for procurement orchestration ([ORO Labs](https://www.orolabs.ai/resources/press-release/oro-labs-raises-100m-series-c)), and Stripe's Bridge acquisition made stablecoin infrastructure a mainstream payments bet ([Stripe](https://stripe.com/newsroom/news/stripe-to-acquire-bridge)). This says "AI does the work" is fundable, but generic infrastructure layers are already crowded.

**Speculation / bet.** By 2028-2029, most large companies will not run one autonomous agent; they will run hundreds of narrow agents embedded in procurement, claims, legal, revenue, treasury, QA, and compliance. The hard problems will not be "can the model read and write?" but "can the organization accept the output as an auditable business record, a payable invoice, a regulatory filing, a supplier approval, a policy decision, or a production release?" The new bottleneck becomes evidence, exception handling, liability allocation, and workflow-specific sign-off.

**Speculation / bet.** The best new companies will look less like dashboards and more like licensed or compliance-grade service firms whose labor is mostly software. They will sell completed packets, reconciled exceptions, defensible decisions, certified records, or regulator-ready evidence. Foundation labs may commoditize generic reasoning, but they are unlikely to own every regulated workflow, data integration, licensing relationship, and budget-specific service wrapper.

## 1. Supplier-bank-change verification desk for agentic procurement

**Problem.** Enterprise procurement and AP teams receive supplier bank changes, tax forms, insurance certificates, sanctions checks, and onboarding updates weekly; one fraudulent or stale supplier record can redirect six- or seven-figure payments.

**Industry & who pays.** Procurement/AP, treasury controls, supplier risk. Buyer: CFO, CPO, controller, shared-services head.

**Why unsolved today.** ERP supplier masters, procurement suites, email, W-9/W-8 forms, COIs, banking portals, and sanctions tools are fragmented. Workarounds are manual callbacks, dual approval, spreadsheet trackers, and quarterly supplier cleanups.

**Why 2028-2029.** Observed: procurement orchestration is attracting capital, with ORO Labs raising $100M for AI procurement workflows ([ORO Labs](https://www.orolabs.ai/resources/press-release/oro-labs-raises-100m-series-c)). Speculation: by 2028, procurement agents will generate and process more supplier-change events, increasing both throughput and fraud surface. The wedge is not "procurement copilot"; it is verified supplier-record change execution with audit trails.

**VC / funding / market signal.** YC asks for AI-native agencies and infrastructure for government fraud hunters, both adjacent to work-execution plus verification ([YC RFS](https://www.ycombinator.com/rfs)). ORO/Zip-style procurement funding validates budget; the gap is supplier-truth operations, not intake UI.

**How it makes money.** Service-as-software: per verified supplier change, per active supplier, and premium SLA for high-risk bank changes. Sell to CFO/CPO through fraud-loss and audit-control pain.

**Strongest counterargument.** ERP/procurement incumbents can bundle this as supplier-risk workflows; banks may own account validation; category could become a feature inside Coupa, SAP Ariba, Zip, or ORO.

**Fit note.** Plausible if framed as trust/security infra for business agents; weaker if it becomes pure procurement implementation consulting.

**Scores.** Unsolvedness 4 - the work exists today and remains manual. Timing 4 - agentic procurement increases volume. Funding momentum 4 - procurement AI is hot. Monetization clarity 5 - fraud loss and audit control are direct. Market budget 5 - CFO/procurement budgets are real. Defensibility-vs-foundation-labs 3 - moat depends on integrations, verification network, and liability workflow.

## 2. Stablecoin payment exception operations for non-crypto enterprises

**Problem.** Exporters, marketplaces, payroll platforms, and cross-border vendors adopting stablecoin rails will face daily exceptions: wrong chain/address, sanctions hits, invoice mismatch, refund disputes, FX/accounting treatment, and unsupported local payout corridors.

**Industry & who pays.** Fintech, treasury, marketplaces, global payroll, B2B payments. Buyer: head of payments, treasurer, CFO, compliance lead.

**Why unsolved today.** Crypto-native tools handle wallets and KYT, while enterprise treasury tools handle bank rails. The messy middle - reconciling stablecoin payments against invoices, tax, refund, sanctions, and accounting systems - is still mostly ops teams plus spreadsheets.

**Why 2028-2029.** Observed: Stripe acquired Bridge for stablecoin infrastructure ([Stripe](https://stripe.com/newsroom/news/stripe-to-acquire-bridge)); YC explicitly asks for stablecoin financial services ([YC RFS](https://www.ycombinator.com/rfs)). Speculation: by 2028, stablecoins are less a crypto product and more a payment rail, creating boring enterprise exception work.

**VC / funding / market signal.** Stripe/Bridge is the clearest validation; YC's stablecoin RFS names the category. Regulatory clarity is improving in the US through stablecoin legislation and agency attention, though exact obligations remain jurisdiction-dependent ([Congress GENIUS Act tracker](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/1582)).

**How it makes money.** Service-as-software exception desk: bps on settled volume plus per-exception resolution fees; enterprise tier for ledger integrations and compliance evidence.

**Strongest counterargument.** Stripe, Bridge, Circle, Fireblocks, banks, and treasury-management systems may own this layer because they already touch the money movement and compliance stack.

**Fit note.** Strong fit with fininfra and AI-native internet interests if built as API-first operations, not a crypto dashboard.

**Scores.** Unsolvedness 4 - enterprise exceptions are not well served by wallet tooling. Timing 5 - stablecoin rails are crossing into mainstream. Funding momentum 5 - huge strategic validation. Monetization clarity 5 - payments ops has clear cost and risk. Market budget 5 - treasury/payment budgets exist. Defensibility-vs-foundation-labs 3 - payments network and compliance integrations matter more than model quality.

## 3. AI-scribe documentation audit defense for hospitals

**Problem.** Hospitals adopting ambient documentation will generate more notes faster, but compliance teams still need to catch unsupported codes, cloned language, missing medical necessity, and payer-audit exposure before claims or regulatory reviews.

**Industry & who pays.** Healthcare provider revenue integrity, compliance, HIM/coding, clinical documentation improvement. Buyer: CFO, chief compliance officer, VP revenue cycle.

**Why unsolved today.** Ambient scribes optimize clinician time; coding and compliance teams still manually sample notes, compare evidence to billed codes, and respond to denials/audits. Workarounds are nurse/coder review queues and retrospective payer appeal packets.

**Why 2028-2029.** Observed: Abridge raised $300M for clinical documentation AI ([Abridge](https://www.abridge.com/press/abridge-raises-300m-series-e)); OpenEvidence raised large rounds in medical AI ([OpenEvidence](https://www.openevidence.com/news)). Speculation: as note volume and AI-authored language rise, payers and regulators will scrutinize whether documentation supports billing.

**VC / funding / market signal.** Healthcare AI funding is very strong, but most visible winners are clinician-facing or knowledge-facing. The gap is downstream evidence defense for revenue/compliance teams.

**How it makes money.** Service-as-software: per audited encounter, percentage of prevented denial/recovered revenue, and enterprise compliance monitoring subscription.

**Strongest counterargument.** Epic, Abridge, Nuance/Microsoft, RCM vendors, and payers can add native audit checks. Also, hospitals may avoid buying anything that appears to encourage upcoding.

**Fit note.** Moderate fit: strong AI-workflow thesis, but healthcare GTM and compliance trust are a stretch.

**Scores.** Unsolvedness 4 - ambient notes create downstream risk. Timing 5 - adoption is accelerating now. Funding momentum 5 - healthcare AI rounds are large. Monetization clarity 4 - denial prevention is measurable. Market budget 5 - revenue integrity budgets are large. Defensibility-vs-foundation-labs 3 - EHR integrations and payer evidence patterns are the moat.

## 4. AI-era litigation privilege and evidence-integrity service

**Problem.** Litigation teams using AI for document review, chronology, and deposition prep need to prove privileged material was not exposed, AI-generated summaries are traceable to source documents, and productions do not include hallucinated or tainted evidence.

**Industry & who pays.** Legal, eDiscovery, corporate litigation, law firms. Buyer: litigation partner, GC, litigation support director.

**Why unsolved today.** eDiscovery systems provide review workflows, but AI summarization and agentic legal work create a new recordkeeping problem: what source did the AI rely on, who saw it, did it cross privilege walls, and can the produced artifact be defended in court? Workarounds are manual privilege logs, review sampling, and locking AI out of sensitive matters.

**Why 2028-2029.** Observed: Harvey raised $300M Series E for legal AI ([Harvey](https://www.harvey.ai/blog/harvey-raises-300m-series-e)); legal AI adoption is pushing beyond drafting into case workflows. Speculation: by 2028, AI-created work product will be common enough that courts and opposing counsel will demand provenance, privilege, and admissibility evidence.

**VC / funding / market signal.** Legal AI is a knife fight at the assistant layer; this is a compliance/evidence service around AI use, not another lawyer copilot.

**How it makes money.** Per-matter service-as-software: privilege-wall monitoring, AI-work-product ledger, production certification, and expert declaration package.

**Strongest counterargument.** Relativity, DISCO, Everlaw, iManage, Microsoft, and Harvey can own this through document-system access. The wedge only works if buyers want an independent defensibility layer.

**Fit note.** Moderate fit as audit/security infra for AI-native work; legal domain credibility is the stretch.

**Scores.** Unsolvedness 4 - provenance for AI legal work is immature. Timing 4 - legal AI is moving into sensitive workflows. Funding momentum 5 - legal AI capital is intense. Monetization clarity 4 - per-matter budgets are high. Market budget 5 - litigation spend is real. Defensibility-vs-foundation-labs 3 - defensibility comes from integrations, chain-of-custody, and legal process.

**Industry & who pays.** Manufacturing, supply chain compliance, automotive, battery, electronics, textiles. Buyer: VP supply chain, head of product compliance, quality/regulatory lead.

**Why unsolved today.** Data lives across PLM, ERP, supplier PDFs, lab reports, spreadsheets, and email. Workarounds are consultant-heavy compliance projects and manual supplier questionnaires.

**Why 2028-2029.** Observed: EU Digital Product Passport obligations are advancing, with batteries among the first categories ([European Commission product passport](https://single-market-economy.ec.europa.eu/single-market/goods/sustainable-products/digital-product-passport_en), [EU Battery Regulation](https://environment.ec.europa.eu/topics/waste-and-recycling/batteries_en)). Speculation: by 2028, product-passport data pipelines become recurring operational requirements, not one-time compliance projects.

**VC / funding / market signal.** YC asks for modern metal mills and physical-work AI, which points at industrial workflows beyond pure SaaS ([YC RFS](https://www.ycombinator.com/rfs)). This is not the prior "food provenance" idea; it is compliance-record assembly for regulated product categories.

**How it makes money.** Service-as-software: per SKU/passport generated and maintained; supplier-network onboarding fees; enterprise compliance dashboards only as evidence, not the core product.

**Strongest counterargument.** PLM/ERP incumbents and compliance platforms may absorb this. Standards may remain fuzzy enough that buyers delay until enforcement bites.

**Fit note.** Stretch: good AI/data extraction problem, but industrial compliance GTM is domain-heavy.

**Scores.** Unsolvedness 4 - data fragmentation is real. Timing 5 - regulation is forcing deadlines. Funding momentum 3 - not as hot as agents, but VC likes industrial AI. Monetization clarity 4 - compliance deadlines create budget. Market budget 4 - manufacturers spend when market access is at risk. Defensibility-vs-foundation-labs 4 - supplier network and compliance templates help.

## 6. AI-data-center load-flexibility and curtailment settlement desk

**Problem.** Colocation operators and large AI compute buyers need to prove, forecast, and financially settle flexible load reductions when grid operators, utilities, or power contracts require curtailment during constrained hours.

**Industry & who pays.** Data centers, energy, utilities, AI infrastructure. Buyer: data-center energy procurement, COO, hyperscaler infrastructure teams, colocation CFO.

**Why unsolved today.** Data centers optimize uptime and power procurement, but AI workloads create a new class of deferrable or migratable compute. Workarounds are static PPAs, manual curtailment events, and overbuilt backup power.

**Why 2028-2029.** Observed: IEA expects data-center and AI electricity demand to grow sharply ([IEA electricity 2024 analysis](https://www.iea.org/reports/electricity-2024)); grid connection and power availability are now board-level bottlenecks. Speculation: by 2028, AI training/inference schedulers can move non-urgent workloads around grid constraints, but somebody must turn that into utility-grade settlement evidence.

**VC / funding / market signal.** Energy and AI infrastructure are attracting capital, but prior digests already covered interconnection/permitting. This is post-connection operating economics: verified load flexibility.

**How it makes money.** Percentage of demand-response/curtailment revenue, SaaS fee per MW managed, and integration projects for workload schedulers plus utility telemetry.

**Strongest counterargument.** Hyperscalers may build this internally; utilities and grid-software incumbents may own settlement; colos may resist anything that touches uptime commitments.

**Fit note.** Stretch but interesting if approached as AI-infra control plane; energy-market sales are hard.

**Scores.** Unsolvedness 4 - flexible AI load is new. Timing 5 - power is the compute bottleneck. Funding momentum 4 - AI infra and energy are both hot. Monetization clarity 4 - savings/revenue are measurable. Market budget 5 - data-center power budgets are enormous. Defensibility-vs-foundation-labs 4 - utility integration and settlement data are not foundation-lab features.

## 7. Regulated manufacturing nonconformance packet automation

**Problem.** Aerospace, defense, medical-device, and auto suppliers generate nonconformances, deviations, CAPAs, first-article reports, and customer quality packets every week, but quality engineers still spend hours assembling evidence from shop-floor systems, drawings, photos, test results, and supplier certs.

**Industry & who pays.** Regulated manufacturing quality. Buyer: VP quality, plant GM, operations head, compliance lead.

**Why unsolved today.** MES/QMS systems record events but do not complete the packet across messy sources. Workarounds are quality engineers copying screenshots into Word/PDF templates, routing emails, and waiting for customer quality sign-off.

**Why 2028-2029.** Observed: FDA's Quality Management System Regulation aligns medical-device QMS with ISO 13485 and takes effect in 2026 ([FDA QMSR](https://www.fda.gov/medical-devices/quality-system-qs-regulationmedical-device-good-manufacturing-practices/quality-management-system-regulation-final-rule-amending-quality-system-regulation)). Speculation: multimodal agents will read drawings, inspection images, CMM outputs, and QMS records well enough to draft defensible packets by 2028.

**VC / funding / market signal.** YC's physical-work and modern manufacturing RFS themes point to industrial workflows ([YC RFS](https://www.ycombinator.com/rfs)). This is service-as-software for quality documentation, not a generic factory copilot.

**How it makes money.** Per packet/CAPA/nonconformance; plant subscription for high-volume lines; premium for customer-specific templates and audit support.

**Strongest counterargument.** QMS incumbents like ETQ, MasterControl, Arena/PTC, Siemens, and SAP can add AI packet generation. Buyers may distrust auto-generated quality records unless human sign-off remains central.

**Fit note.** Moderate stretch: good multimodal agent workflow; manufacturing trust and field sales are the hard part.

**Scores.** Unsolvedness 4 - packet assembly is still labor-heavy. Timing 4 - multimodal/document agents are improving. Funding momentum 3 - industrial AI is less crowded but less flashy. Monetization clarity 5 - engineer hours and delayed shipments are measurable. Market budget 4 - quality budgets exist in regulated plants. Defensibility-vs-foundation-labs 4 - plant integrations, templates, and audit history matter.

**How it makes money.** Service-as-software casefile generation: per investigation packet, agency subscription, implementation through systems integrators, optional recovery-based economics where legal.

**Strongest counterargument.** Palantir, Thomson Reuters, LexisNexis Risk, SAS, and incumbent case-management vendors can dominate. Public procurement may be too slow for startup velocity.

**Fit note.** Plausible if built as fraud/security infrastructure; public-sector GTM is the stretch.

**Scores.** Unsolvedness 4 - detection-to-casefile remains manual. Timing 4 - AI-generated fraud raises volume. Funding momentum 4 - YC is explicitly asking. Monetization clarity 3 - government sales are slow but budgets exist. Market budget 4 - fraud recovery has clear value. Defensibility-vs-foundation-labs 4 - legal workflows, data access, and agency trust matter.

## 9. Commercial insurance AI-exposure underwriting workbench

**Problem.** Commercial insurers and brokers must understand whether a business's AI agents can give advice, make hiring/credit/medical decisions, move money, publish content, or control operations, but today those exposures are buried in questionnaires, vendor contracts, logs, and policy language.

**Industry & who pays.** Commercial insurance, brokers, cyber/E&O/D&O underwriting. Buyer: carrier underwriting head, broker practice lead, risk engineering leader.

**Why unsolved today.** Carriers ask broad cyber/technology questions, but agentic AI creates new liability paths that do not map cleanly to existing underwriting forms. Workarounds are manual supplemental questionnaires, exclusions, and conservative pricing.

**Why 2028-2029.** Observed: insurance regulators are formalizing governance expectations for insurer AI use, such as the NAIC model bulletin on AI systems ([NAIC AI model bulletin](https://content.naic.org/sites/default/files/inline-files/2023%20AI%20Model%20Bulletin_Adopted_0.pdf)). Speculation: by 2028, carriers will need to underwrite customers' AI-agent exposure, not just their own model governance.

**VC / funding / market signal.** Prior reports rejected a broad actuarial reliability data layer. This sharper wedge sells underwriting work product: account triage, endorsements/exclusions, risk-control recommendations, and renewal evidence.

**How it makes money.** Per account assessment for brokers/carriers; enterprise subscription for portfolio monitoring; revenue share with risk-control services.

**Strongest counterargument.** Cyber insurers, brokers, and risk-engineering teams may build their own checklists. The category may be too early until enough AI-agent claims exist.

**Fit note.** Stronger than it looks: it intersects agent risk, finance, security, and B2B evidence. Insurance domain is the learning curve.

**Scores.** Unsolvedness 4 - existing questionnaires are not agent-specific. Timing 4 - AI liability exposure will surface by renewal cycles. Funding momentum 3 - insurance AI is quieter than healthcare/legal. Monetization clarity 4 - underwriting and risk-control budgets exist. Market budget 5 - commercial insurance is moneyed. Defensibility-vs-foundation-labs 4 - policy language, claims data, and broker distribution are moats.

## 10. AI-native hedge-fund compliance and strategy-control plane

**Problem.** Small funds and family offices experimenting with AI-generated trading strategies need daily proof that strategies obey mandates, concentration limits, data-use restrictions, backtest controls, trade surveillance rules, and investor disclosures.

**Industry & who pays.** Asset management, hedge funds, family offices, quant funds. Buyer: COO, CCO, portfolio manager, fund administrator.

**Why unsolved today.** Compliance systems monitor trades and communications, while research platforms track notebooks/backtests. AI agents blur research, signal generation, code generation, and trade execution. Workarounds are human CCO review, pre-trade rules, and restricting agents to research-only mode.

**Why 2028-2029.** Observed: YC asks for AI-native hedge funds ([YC RFS](https://www.ycombinator.com/rfs)); frontier coding/data agents are improving. Speculation: by 2028, many funds will use agents to generate, test, and deploy strategies, creating a need for a compliance-control layer that understands both model lineage and trading constraints.

**VC / funding / market signal.** This is a derivative pick from YC's RFS: if AI-native hedge funds are buildable, the picks-and-shovels include strategy governance, investor evidence, and automated compliance review.

**How it makes money.** High-ACV SaaS plus implementation; per strategy under governance; optional managed compliance review for emerging managers.

**Strongest counterargument.** Established OMS/EMS, surveillance, and compliance vendors can add AI-strategy governance. Funds may keep this internal because it reveals alpha and trading logic.

**Fit note.** Strong fit with finance infra and frontier-agent leverage, though regulated-finance credibility is required.

**Scores.** Unsolvedness 4 - AI strategy lineage is not handled by standard compliance. Timing 4 - depends on AI-native funds actually proliferating. Funding momentum 4 - YC signal is explicit. Monetization clarity 5 - funds pay for compliance and risk controls. Market budget 4 - smaller than healthcare but high willingness to pay. Defensibility-vs-foundation-labs 4 - alpha confidentiality and workflow integrations protect against generic labs.
