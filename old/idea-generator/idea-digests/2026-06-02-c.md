Third run today. Deliberately steered *away* from the agent-reliability / eval /

## Step 2 — Forecast: the AI-shaped world of 2028-2029

**Observed today (signal).** Inference is cheap and getting cheaper — GPT-4-level
capability fell from ~$30/M tokens in early 2023 to under $1/M now, and frontier
agentic models sit at $1.75–$5/M input. The 80%-reliability "time horizon" for
autonomous tasks has been *doubling every ~4.7 months* since reasoning models
appeared, with the newest models beating even that curve. Capital is concentrating
into fewer, larger bets: OpenAI's ~$122B round, Anthropic's $65B, and vertical
"service-as-software" companies selling completed work — Harvey at ~$190M ARR,
Legora's $550M Series D, Sierra at a $15B valuation. The explicit VC asks have moved
from "AI features" to "rebuild the whole industry": YC's 2026 RFS wants AI-native
hedge funds, agencies, government, metal mills, modern agriculture, counter-drone,
chip-supply tooling and stablecoin finance; Sequoia's 2026 thesis is long-horizon
agents + AI-native services (work budgets ~6× software budgets); a16z's Big Ideas
flag an "agent-speed traffic" infrastructure shock and AI-delivered professional
services.

**Extrapolation to 2028-2029 (speculation, labeled).** If the time-horizon curve
even half-holds, by 2028-2029 a meaningful share of multi-hour knowledge work
(claims, filings, estimates, eligibility reviews) is executed by agents with humans
on exception-handling, and the dominant software *shape* is outcome-priced
service-as-software rather than seat-priced SaaS. That creates a predictable class
of *second-order* problems that barely exist today: (1) backends and networks built
for human-speed traffic get hammered by recursive agent fan-out that looks like a
self-inflicted DDoS; (2) money, liability, and consent move through agents faster
than the dispute/arbitration rails that govern them; (3) regulation (EU AI Act
Article 50, US state AI laws, the Take It Down Act) forces *provenance and disclosure*
on every piece of machine-made content; and (4) the unglamorous, document-choked,
low-AI industries — construction, healthcare RCM, government adjudication — finally
have cheap-enough, reliable-enough agents to attack their paperwork, but the hard
part becomes *clinical/legal/contractual nuance and defensibility*, not the model.

**The derivation.** The best fresh bets this run aren't "a better agent" — they're
the **rails, governance, and outcome-delivery layers** that an agent-saturated world
needs and doesn't have. Below: six, across six industries.

## 1. Agent-speed backend traffic governance — the "internal DDoS" control plane

**Problem.** When an enterprise puts an autonomous agent in front of its own
backend, a single user goal can fan out into thousands of recursive sub-tasks,
database queries, and internal API calls in milliseconds. To a legacy rate-limiter
or database, that pattern is indistinguishable from a DDoS attack — except it's
coming from inside, from a sanctioned agent. Today there is no control plane that
shapes, prioritizes, and budgets *agent-originated* internal traffic by goal/intent
rather than by IP or per-key quota.

**Industry & who pays.** AI-native internet / platform infra. Buyers: platform and
SRE/infra teams at any company deploying internal or customer-facing agents against
their own services (mid-to-large SaaS, fintech, e-commerce).

**Why unsolved today.** Existing tools split the problem badly: API gateways and
WAFs reason about clients, not *intents*; observability shows the fan-out after it
hurts; agent FinOps tools (covered in prior digests) cap *spend*, not backend load
or contention. Nobody offers admission-control + intent-aware shaping that
understands "these 5,000 calls belong to one agent goal, throttle as a unit, and
shed the low-value branches first." Worked around today by crude global rate limits
that throttle humans too, or by over-provisioning.

**Why 2028-2029.** Signal: a16z explicitly names "agent-speed" workloads
(recursive, bursty, massive) as 2026's biggest infrastructure shock, and bot/automation
traffic is reportedly growing ~8× faster than human traffic with APIs the dominant
surface. Speculation: as agent concurrency rises another order of magnitude, backend
contention — not token cost — becomes the binding constraint, and a dedicated layer
gets bought rather than hand-rolled.

**VC / funding / market signal.** a16z Big Ideas 2026 (Part 1) frames this exact
shock; HUMAN Security and Imperva 2026 reports quantify the automation-traffic surge
and API-attack concentration. No RFS names it directly — early, but pointed.

**How it makes money.** Usage/throughput-based infra pricing (per protected request
or per agent-fleet), OSS core → enterprise control plane, sold to platform teams.
Not service-as-software; this is picks-and-shovels.

**Strongest counterargument.** A frontier lab or the hyperscalers/CDNs (Cloudflare,
Akamai, the cloud API gateways) are the natural owners — Cloudflare already ships
agent/bot-management primitives and could extend "agent traffic shaping" as a
feature, swallowing a startup. The moat (deep intent-graph understanding of agent
fan-out) is real but may be a feature, not a company.

**Fit note.** Strong fit — this sits squarely in my agent-infra / reliability edge
(token budgeting, multi-provider routing, distributed long-horizon execution) and is
sold founder-led to technical buyers. The most "me" idea on the list.

**Scores.** unsolvedness 4 (no intent-aware layer exists) · timing 3 (real but maybe
a year early) · funding momentum 3 (thesis-level, not yet a funded category) ·
monetization clarity 4 (clean usage infra pricing) · market budget 4 (infra budgets
are large and growing) · defensibility-vs-labs 2 (CDN/cloud incumbents are the
threat, not the labs).

## 2. Prior-authorization & RCM service-as-software with clinical-nuance reliability

**Problem.** A US health system's staff spend ~14.5 hours/week per physician on
prior authorizations; 93% of physicians say PA delays care and ~⅓ report it caused a
serious adverse event. The industry burns an estimated $35B/year on it. Early AI
tools speed individual steps but, per a payer-funded study, *raise* transaction
volume and cost-per-claim and still choke on the clinical nuance of an encounter —
so providers get faster paperwork, not lower net cost or fewer wrongful denials.

**Industry & who pays.** Healthcare back-office (provider-side RCM). Buyers: hospital
systems, large physician groups, and RCM outsourcers — budget holders who currently
pay people or BPOs by the claim.

**Why unsolved today.** The hard part isn't form-filling; it's reliably reading the
clinical record, mapping it to a specific payer's shifting policy, assembling
defensible medical-necessity evidence, and contesting denials — with an audit trail
a payer and a compliance officer will accept. Worked around with offshore BPO teams,
point "copilots," and brute-force resubmission.

**Why 2028-2029.** Signal: agentic RCM vendors (UiPath, ZeOmega in Gartner's 2026
guide) are emerging; reported 30–40% admin-labor reductions where deployed.
Speculation: as long-horizon agents cross the reliability bar for multi-document
clinical reasoning, the model shifts from "tool we license" to "denials handled,
priced per won authorization."

**VC / funding / market signal.** Open Evidence ($250M at $12B, 700k physicians)
shows capital and adoption in clinical-decision AI; PHTI/Fierce Healthcare and HFMA
document both the $35B pain and the "AI raises cost" gap. No single RFS, but YC's
"AI-native agencies/services" and Sequoia's services thesis cover the shape.

**How it makes money.** Service-as-software: outcome pricing per approved
authorization / per recovered denial, sold to RCM leaders, displacing BPO spend.

**Strongest counterargument.** This is already a knife fight — Epic/athenahealth,
the payers themselves, UiPath, and a dozen funded RCM-AI startups are in it, and the
real moat (payer policy data + clinical-nuance reliability + EHR integrations) favors
incumbents with distribution and data. A newcomer with no healthcare credibility or
EHR partnerships likely loses on go-to-market, not technology.

**Fit note.** Stretch — no healthcare domain depth or buyer relationships, though the
reliability/eval/audit-trail core is exactly my edge. Would need a clinical co-founder.

**Scores.** unsolvedness 3 (worked-on, badly) · timing 4 (regulatory + cost pressure
acute now) · funding momentum 4 (heavy) · monetization clarity 5 (per-outcome,
displaces a known budget) · market budget 5 ($35B+) · defensibility-vs-labs 3 (labs
won't, but incumbents will).

## 3. Construction document service-as-software — RFIs, submittals, change orders done

**Problem.** Construction is drowning in documents: a single project team loses
1–2 full-time-equivalents to RFI tracking (4–6 hrs/wk), submittal coordination
(3–5 hrs/wk), and status reporting — and unanswered RFIs or missed submittals turn
into costly disputes and change orders. Only 27% of AEC professionals use AI at all,
and ~52% still use paper in design. The pain is severe, frequent, and quantifiable.

**Industry & who pays.** Construction (GCs, subs, owners' reps). Budget holders:
general contractors and project executives who today staff coordinators or eat the
rework cost.

**Why unsolved today.** Legacy workflows are email + shared drives + tracking
spreadsheets; incumbents (Procore/Autodesk) are systems-of-record, not autonomous
*doers*. The hard part is reliably reading drawings + specs + contracts together,
catching the conflict before it becomes a claim, and producing a defensible paper
trail — exactly where generic copilots are shallow.

**Why 2028-2029.** Signal: Autodesk's 2026 survey names document/RFI management the
highest-impact in-production AI use; contech funding is flowing (six startups raised
$126M in early 2026; Zero RFI $13.8M; XBuild $19M Series A for chat-based estimating).
Speculation: as multimodal models reliably reason over drawings + specs, the wedge
moves from "copilot" to "submittal/RFI loop closed for you," priced per project.

**VC / funding / market signal.** Concrete and recent: Zero RFI ($13.8M), XBuild
($19M), $126M across six contech startups in early 2026 (Construction Owners /
constructionowners.com). Validation — and a crowding warning.

**How it makes money.** Per-project or per-seat-plus-outcome SaaS; service-as-software
variant: priced per RFI/submittal cycle resolved. Sold through GC project teams.

**Strongest counterargument.** Procore and Autodesk own the data and the distribution;
the moment AI doc-handling is proven, they bundle it into the system of record buyers
already pay for, and a standalone startup becomes a feature. Plus the named startups
(Zero RFI, XBuild) already have a head start in the exact wedge.

**Fit note.** Stretch — no construction network or domain depth; this is far from my
security/agent-infra edge and would be a cold-start in an unfamiliar buyer world.

**Scores.** unsolvedness 3 · timing 4 · funding momentum 4 · monetization clarity 4 ·
market budget 4 (huge industry, low-but-rising willingness to pay) · defensibility-vs-labs
3 (threat is incumbents/contech startups, not labs).

## 4. The consent-and-dispute rail for agentic commerce (not fraud detection — arbitration)

**Problem.** Agents are starting to buy. The payment authorization rails are nearly
ready (Visa Intelligent Commerce, Mastercard Agent Pay), but the *dispute* rails are
not: when an agent buys the wrong thing, or a user claims they never authorized a
purchase the agent made, today's chargeback framework can't fairly arbitrate
*"when was authority granted and did the agent act within it?"* Merchants can eat
chargebacks even when the consumer authorized the agent. Without a consent/permission
record embedded in the transaction, agentic disputes are "almost impossible to
arbitrate fairly."

**Industry & who pays.** Payments / fintech. Buyers: merchants and PSPs/acquirers
exposed to agent-initiated chargeback liability; secondarily issuers needing
evidence to adjudicate.

**Why unsolved today.** Card networks are shipping *authorization* tokens and
"verifiable intent" pilots, but the cross-party, tamper-evident *consent ledger +
dispute-evidence + arbitration workflow* layer barely exists in production. Worked
around with manual merchant chargeback teams and liability-neutral guesswork.
(Distinct from the prior digest's merchant-side *fraud-detection* idea — this is the
post-hoc consent-record and dispute-resolution layer, not real-time fraud scoring.)

**Why 2028-2029.** Signal: multiple 2026 sources (Chargebacks911, Rivero,
Electronic Payments International, Checkout.com) independently flag the same gap —
"payments are ready, disputes are not." Mastercard's "verifiable intent" is nascent.
Speculation: as agent purchase volume scales, dispute volume scales with it and a
neutral evidence/arbitration standard becomes mandatory infrastructure.

**VC / funding / market signal.** YC RFS 2026 explicitly wants stablecoin financial
services; a16z crypto argues agentic commerce "opens a gap" cards don't fill. The
dispute layer is the gap the payments press keeps naming.

**How it makes money.** Per-dispute or per-transaction fee on a consent-record /
arbitration network; could ride card rails (TAP/AP2) and stablecoin settlement (x402).
Network-effect business if it becomes the neutral standard.

**Strongest counterargument.** This is network-economics turf — Visa and Mastercard
are *already* building the authorization and "verifiable intent" primitives, and
disputes are historically owned by the networks and issuers. A startup defining a
neutral standard between them faces the classic two-sided cold-start plus the risk
the networks simply extend their own rails to cover it.

**Fit note.** Stretch-to-moderate — adjacent to my fintech buyer credibility and the
trust/verification + audit-trail core is my wheelhouse, but I have no payments-network
relationships and this is a standards/distribution game more than a reliability game.

**Scores.** unsolvedness 4 (clear, named gap) · timing 3 (real but dispute volume
still small) · funding momentum 3 (auth funded, disputes not yet) · monetization
clarity 3 (network pricing, cold-start) · market budget 4 (payments dispute spend is
large) · defensibility-vs-labs 4 (labs irrelevant; networks are the threat).

## 5. AI-content provenance, labeling & disclosure compliance layer (the "compliance splinternet" toolkit)

**Problem.** Starting in 2026, an enterprise that generates or publishes AI content
must *prove* provenance and disclosure across a fragmenting patchwork of law, or face
liability it "cannot document its way out of." EU AI Act Article 50 transparency/
synthetic-content labeling becomes enforceable Aug 2, 2026; California AB 2013
(training-data disclosure) and SB 942 (AI-content labeling) are live as of Jan 1,
2026; the federal Take It Down Act (48-hour NCII/deepfake removal) takes effect
May 19, 2026; 36 states have active AI bills. Same feature, different legality per
jurisdiction — a "compliance splinternet."

**Industry & who pays.** Media / marketing / platforms / regtech. Buyers: marketing
and content orgs at large brands, ad agencies, publishers, and UGC platforms that
must label, watermark, log provenance, and honor takedowns.

**Why unsolved today.** Watermarking standards (C2PA) exist but enforcement,
cross-jurisdiction policy mapping, content-level provenance logging, and the
takedown-SLA workflow are stitched together by hand or by lawyers. Worked around with
legal review and manual labeling — doesn't scale to machine-generated volume.
(Distinct from the prior digest's EU-AI-Act *Article 12 agent-reasoning* evidence
idea; this is Article 50 / state-law *content provenance and disclosure*.)

**Why 2028-2029.** Signal: hard statutory dates in 2026 (above) with escalating
state activity; "provenance tagging" gaps already named as un-documentable liability.
Speculation: by 2028-2029, content provenance is a default compliance line-item the
way cookie-consent became after GDPR.

**VC / funding / market signal.** Driven by regulation rather than a single RFS —
EU AI Act (Art. 50), CA AB 2013 / SB 942, the Take It Down Act, and the
36-state bill landscape (Kiteworks, MultiState, Jones Walker, Baker Botts 2026 trackers).

**How it makes money.** SaaS + per-asset provenance/labeling and compliance-as-a-service;
audit-ready logs sold to legal/compliance and marketing ops. Possible service-as-software:
"disclosure handled, takedowns met within SLA."

**Strongest counterargument.** Timing and fragmentation cut both ways — US federal
posture on AI rules is volatile and could preempt or gut state laws, collapsing the
demand; and the foundation-model labs + platforms (Adobe/C2PA, the model providers
themselves) may bake provenance/watermarking in at the source, leaving little for a
third party. Regulation-driven markets also move on political timelines, not product ones.

**Fit note.** Stretch — compliance/regtech is outside my security-but-not-policy
edge, and the buyer (legal/marketing) isn't my technical-buyer comfort zone.

**Scores.** unsolvedness 4 · timing 4 (statutory dates in 2026) · funding momentum 2
(quiet so far) · monetization clarity 3 · market budget 3 (compliance budgets exist
but new) · defensibility-vs-labs 2 (labs/platforms may own provenance at source).

## 6. Claimant-side adjudication service-as-software for government benefits (disability/SSI/Medicaid)

**Problem.** The SSA initial-disability backlog sits around ~831–865K pending cases
with 6–8 month initial processing times; claimants and the advocates/firms who
represent them must assemble medical evidence, navigate shifting eligibility rules,
and contest denials with almost no automation. SSA itself has said it will *not*
lean on AI to fix the backlog (citing nuance and staffing), leaving the *claimant
side* — disability law firms, advocates, and benefits navigators — underserved.

**Industry & who pays.** Govtech / legal-services-adjacent. Buyers: disability law
firms and benefits-advocacy practices (paid on contingency from awarded back-pay),
and potentially state/county benefits navigators.

**Why unsolved today.** It's document- and rule-heavy multi-month casework — exactly
what was too unreliable to automate until long-horizon agents. The agency won't build
it; incumbents serve the agency, not claimants. Worked around with paralegals and
manual evidence assembly.

**Why 2028-2029.** Signal: concrete backlog figures and the March 2026 SSA DDS→DCR
restructuring (SSA press release) show the system in flux and starved of capacity;
SSA's stated reluctance to use AI internally creates a durable third-party opening.
Speculation: agents reliable enough for multi-document medical-eligibility reasoning
make per-case automation viable for the representation side by 2028-2029.

**VC / funding / market signal.** YC RFS 2026 explicitly lists "AI for Government";
the NASI AI-and-disability-benefits task-force work and KFF/SSA data quantify the
backlog. No marquee funded competitor surfaced — possibly an open field.

**How it makes money.** Service-as-software priced per case won / share of recovered
back-pay (contingency-aligned), or per-seat for law firms. Sales motion: founder-led
into disability-law practices.

**Strongest counterargument.** The market may be structurally small and slow:
contingency fees are capped, sales into small law firms is a grind, and any tool that
touches benefit determinations carries acute regulatory/ethical risk (a wrong denial
harms a vulnerable claimant). It may be a worthy services business but not venture-scale,
and political winds around benefits programs add policy risk.

**Fit note.** Stretch — no legal/govtech network, and the regulated-but-low-margin
shape is a poor match for my "venture-scale, technical-buyer" preference.

**Scores.** unsolvedness 4 (claimant side largely untouched) · timing 3 · funding
momentum 2 (quiet) · monetization clarity 3 (contingency-aligned but capped) · market
budget 3 · defensibility-vs-labs 4 (labs/agency won't touch the claimant side).

## Shortlist — top 2 by raw opportunity (ignoring fit)

1. **#2 Prior-auth / RCM service-as-software.** Biggest verified budget ($35B/yr of
pure waste), acute and worsening pain, clear per-outcome monetization, and the model
finally crossing the reliability bar. It's a knife fight, but the prize is enormous
and the "AI raises cost / misses clinical nuance" gap means the current solutions are
demonstrably inadequate — room for someone who actually nails reliability and denial
defense.

2. **#1 Agent-speed backend traffic governance.** Highest *newness* — a problem that
essentially doesn't exist yet at scale but is dead-ahead if the agent-concurrency
curve holds, named by a16z as 2026's biggest infra shock. Riskier on timing and on
incumbent (CDN/cloud) capture, but if it becomes a category, it's foundational
picks-and-shovels with durable usage-based economics.

## One pick best-matched to me

**#1 Agent-speed backend traffic governance.** It's the only idea on the list that
lands directly on my sharpest edge — long-horizon agent execution, token/traffic
budgeting, multi-provider routing, and the unglamorous reliability layer that makes
agent fleets dependable. It's B2B, sold founder-led to technical/platform buyers,
fits an OSS-core → enterprise usage motion, and grows *with* AI capability rather
than against it (the more capable agents get, the worse the fan-out problem). Main
thing to de-risk before committing: whether Cloudflare/cloud gateways turn this into
a feature before it can become a company — worth 2–3 design-partner conversations
with infra teams already feeling the fan-out pain.
