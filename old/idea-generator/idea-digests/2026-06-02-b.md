# Idea digest — 2026-06-02 (run b)

*Industry-agnostic hunt. This run deliberately breaks out of the agent-reliability/security
cluster that dominated 2026-06-01 (a–c) and 2026-06-02 (run a). Six ideas span energy,
defense/industrial, insurance, agriculture/food, semiconductor supply chain, and the
hard-tech labor market. Profile used only as an end tiebreaker, never as a filter.*

---

## Step 2 — Forecast: the AI-shaped world of 2028-2029

The cleanest observed signal this cycle is that **the binding constraint on AI has moved from
model quality to the physical world.** Hyperscalers are committing >$1T of capex across
2025-2026, and 78% of built-environment venture went to data centers in 2025 — yet a new
data center takes 12-18 months to build and **five to seven years to connect to the grid**
([Ropes & Gray](https://www.ropesgray.com/en/insights/viewpoints/102mvfl/data-center-investment-in-2026-ai-demand-power-constraints-and-private-equity), [WEF](https://www.weforum.org/stories/2026/05/electricity-data-grid-connectivity-strategic-bottleneck-ai-transformation/)). YC's Summer 2026 RFS abandoned its software heritage to ask for
agriculture robots, counter-drone defense, semiconductor supply-chain software, and lunar
manufacturing — "software loses its moat" ([TNW](https://thenextweb.com/news/yc-summer-2026-rfs-hard-tech-pivot)). a16z's Big Ideas 2026 echoes it: energy,
manufacturing, logistics, and infrastructure "back in focus," AI-native and software-first
([a16z](https://a16z.com/newsletter/big-ideas-2026-part-1/)). So by 2028-2029 the world is *compute-rich but power-, supply-chain-, and
trust-poor.*

On capability: METR's time-horizon metric has a post-2023 doubling time of ~4.3 months, and
the SOTA model (May 2026) has a 50% time horizon of ~16 hours but an 80% horizon of only ~3
hours ([METR](https://metr.org/time-horizons/)). Extrapolated to 2028-2029, agents will reliably (80%) handle multi-day
software-shaped work — which means **service-as-software eats white-collar back offices**
(Gartner: ≥15% of day-to-day decisions made autonomously by 2028, [via William Blair](https://www.williamblair.com/Insights/The-Agentic-Era-Vertical-Softwares-New-Growth-Engine)). But the
reliability-vs-capability gap *widens with task duration*, so the highest-stakes physical and
regulated workflows stay human-gated longest. That gap is where the durable problems live.

The derived problem spaces, then, cluster around three things the 2028-2029 world will be
short of: **(1) electrons and the bureaucracy to deliver them** (grid, interconnection,
permitting); **(2) physical-world supply, provenance, and defense** (chips, drones, food,
reshored manufacturing); and **(3) the new back-office workflows that get automated but stay
regulated** (insurance claims, etc.). Separately, AI adoption itself creates a labor
second-order problem: the hard-tech pivot needs skilled physical-world operators that the
labor market isn't producing. Below, OBSERVED signal is cited; everything about 2028-2029 is
labeled SPECULATION.

---

## 1. Interconnection & permitting automation for power projects ("service-as-software" for the queue)

- **Problem.** A developer trying to energize a new gas plant, substation, battery site, or
  behind-the-meter data-center tie-in spends years assembling interconnection applications,
  interconnection studies, and environmental/permitting filings across balkanized utility and
  ISO processes. The queue, not the steel, is the bottleneck — 5-7 years vs 12-18 months to
  build ([Ropes & Gray](https://www.ropesgray.com/en/insights/viewpoints/102mvfl/data-center-investment-in-2026-ai-demand-power-constraints-and-private-equity)). Every project, every market, repeated, with seven-figure delay costs.
- **Industry & who pays.** Energy / data-center development. Budget holders: IPPs, utilities,
  hyperscaler energy teams, and developers — people sitting on stranded capital while they
  wait.
- **Why unsolved today.** Worked around with boutique consultants and in-house regulatory
  staff who hand-fill study packages. No software *does the filing work*; existing tools are
  GIS/siting maps, not the labor.
- **Why 2028-2029.** The interconnection backlog is structural and growing with AI load
  (SPECULATION: federal pressure to fast-track will *change* the rules repeatedly, raising the
  value of software that re-derives compliant filings each time). OBSERVED: GridCARE raised a
  $64M oversubscribed Series A and Soma Energy $7M to accelerate time-to-power
  ([Latitude](https://www.latitudemedia.com/news/gridcare-raises-oversubscribed-64-million-series-a/), [DCD](https://www.datacenterdynamics.com/en/news/soma-energy-raises-7m-to-speed-data-center-grid-connections-with-ai/)).
- **VC / funding / market signal.** GridCARE $64M Series A; Soma $7M; data centers = 78% of
  built-environment venture in 2025 ([Ropes & Gray](https://www.ropesgray.com/en/insights/viewpoints/102mvfl/data-center-investment-in-2026-ai-demand-power-constraints-and-private-equity)). a16z explicitly names energy/infrastructure as
  AI-native focus ([a16z](https://a16z.com/newsletter/big-ideas-2026-part-1/)).
- **How it makes money.** Service-as-software: per-project fee or % of avoided delay-cost, plus
  SaaS seat for developer teams. Sales motion: founder-led into IPP/utility regulatory teams.
- **Strongest counterargument.** This is deeply relationship- and jurisdiction-bound; the moat
  is regulatory data and utility relationships, not AI — and incumbents (GridCARE, the big
  engineering/permitting consultancies) are better-positioned. A frontier lab won't do this,
  but a well-capitalized energy-software player will, and you'd be the outsider.
- **Fit note.** Stretch. Strong service-as-software shape, but zero domain depth in energy
  regulation and no utility network — a knife fight in someone else's domain.
- **Scores.** Unsolvedness 4 (queue genuinely unautomated) · Timing 5 (acute now) · Funding
  momentum 4 (capital flowing) · Monetization 4 (clear delay-cost ROI) · Market budget 5
  (stranded $B) · Defensibility-vs-labs 5 (labs irrelevant, but human incumbents strong).

## 2. Counter-drone fire-control & airspace-deconfliction software (the OS, not the interceptor)

- **Problem.** Operators defending a base, border, port, or critical-infra site against cheap
  autonomous drone swarms have hardware (jammers, interceptors, sensors) but no reliable
  software layer to fuse sensors, classify threats, and coordinate response across vendors in
  real time without fratricide or jamming friendly comms. The threat is swarm-scale, jam-
  resistant, and lethal ([YC RFS](https://thenextweb.com/news/yc-summer-2026-rfs-hard-tech-pivot)).
- **Industry & who pays.** Defense / critical-infrastructure security. Budget holder: DoD
  (FY2026 budget reached $1T; procuring 200,000 autonomous systems) and allied/critical-infra
  operators ([GlobeNewswire](https://www.globenewswire.com/news-release/2026/04/09/3271006/0/en/The-Pentagon-Wants-200-000-Autonomous-Systems-A-Latin-American-Government-Just-Signed-Its-First-Defense-Order-And-a-Nasdaq-Defense-Tech-Company-Just-Walked-580-Investors-Through-It.html)).
- **Why unsolved today.** Worked around with stovepiped single-vendor systems and human
  operators in the loop. Vendor lock and classification slow an open fire-control fabric.
- **Why 2028-2029.** OBSERVED: YC named counter-drone defense a Summer 2026 RFS; Pentagon
  backed Perennial Autonomy with a $500M counter-drone deal; ParallaxOS/Surtr building open
  drone-defense OS ([DroneLife](https://dronelife.com/2026/05/21/perennial-autonomy-pentagon-contract/), [YC/LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/y-combinator_surtr-defense-systems-yc-p26-is-building-activity-7460403503870504960-_nOu)). SPECULATION: drone proliferation makes this a
  standing domestic need, not just a war function, by 2028.
- **VC / funding / market signal.** $500M Pentagon counter-drone contract; explicit YC RFS;
  active YC defense/drone cohort.
- **How it makes money.** DoD program-of-record + per-site licensing to critical-infra; classic
  defense-procurement motion (long, relationship-heavy).
- **Strongest counterargument.** Defense procurement is slow, cleared-personnel-gated, and
  Anduril-class incumbents plus the new $500M-funded players are already here. As a solo
  foreign-national founder with no clearance or primes relationship, the barrier is structural,
  not technical.
- **Fit note.** Stretch (hard). Reliable-autonomy edge is real and relevant, but defense
  access/clearance constraints are close to disqualifying for this founder specifically.
- **Scores.** Unsolvedness 4 · Timing 5 · Funding momentum 5 · Monetization 4 · Market budget 5
  · Defensibility-vs-labs 5 (labs won't; primes will).

## 3. Autonomous insurance-claims adjudication with built-in audit trail (service-as-software for P&C/health back office)

- **Problem.** A mid-tier insurer's claims back office still routes routine P&C and health
  claims through manual adjusters: intake, document parsing, coverage determination, fraud
  check, payout. It's high-volume, rules-heavy, regulated, and expensive — and the
  determination must be explainable to regulators on demand. Happens millions of times a year.
- **Industry & who pays.** Insurance. Budget holder: carriers and TPAs (third-party
  administrators) with large adjuster payrolls.
- **Why unsolved today.** Worked around with offshore BPO and legacy rules engines. Incumbent
  AI tools do customer-service chat or single-step extraction, not end-to-end *adjudicate-and-
  defend* ([PYMNTS](https://www.pymnts.com/artificial-intelligence-2/2026/ai-agents-are-now-running-the-back-office-at-insurance-giants/)).
- **Why 2028-2029.** OBSERVED: AI agents already running back office at insurance giants; Notch
  raised $30M Series A for autonomous agents in regulated industries; Gartner says ≥15% of
  decisions autonomous by 2028 ([CMSWire](https://www.cmswire.com/customer-experience/notch-raises-30m-series-a-for-regulated-ai-platform/), [PYMNTS](https://www.pymnts.com/artificial-intelligence-2/2026/ai-agents-are-now-running-the-back-office-at-insurance-giants/)). SPECULATION: by 2028 80%-reliability multi-step
  agents make full adjudication defensible.
- **VC / funding / market signal.** Notch $30M Series A (regulated agents); William Blair names
  insurance claims handling as the service-as-software prize with 70-80% gross margins
  ([William Blair](https://www.williamblair.com/Insights/The-Agentic-Era-Vertical-Softwares-New-Growth-Engine)).
- **How it makes money.** Per-claim outcome pricing or % of adjuster cost saved; lands as a
  co-pilot, expands to autonomous. Service-as-software, sticky once integrated.
- **Strongest counterargument.** Crowded and incumbent-favored: Notch, Applied Systems, giant
  carriers building in-house, and vertical insurtechs are already here. The moat is carrier
  data access and compliance trust — exactly what an outsider lacks — and a foundation lab +
  a systems integrator could assemble this faster.
- **Fit note.** Moderate-stretch. The *reliability-and-audit-trail* layer leans on his agent-
  evals/observability edge, and "explainable autonomous decision in a regulated domain" is his
  sweet spot — but he has no insurance buyer network.
- **Scores.** Unsolvedness 3 (partly solved) · Timing 4 · Funding momentum 5 · Monetization 5 ·
  Market budget 5 · Defensibility-vs-labs 3 (incumbents + integrators threaten).

## 4. Residue & provenance verification for the food supply chain (the "is this actually clean?" layer)

- **Problem.** As agriculture shifts away from chemical-heavy practice, buyers (retailers,
  food brands, regulators) have no cheap, continuous way to *verify* pesticide-residue and
  provenance claims on incoming produce — testing is slow, batch-based lab work, and "organic"
  / "low-residue" labels are largely trust-based. Every shipment, every supplier.
- **Industry & who pays.** Agriculture / food. Budget holders: grocery retailers, CPG brands,
  and food importers who carry liability for residue and fraud.
- **Why unsolved today.** Worked around with periodic lab sampling and paper certificates. No
  continuous, AI-fused (spectral + supply-chain-data) verification feeding the buyer's intake.
- **Why 2028-2029.** OBSERVED: YC Summer 2026 RFS explicitly flags pesticide residue
  "everywhere in food, water, and soil" and glyphosate health concern as a fundable space
  ([YC via search](https://www.thevccorner.com/p/yc-summer-2026-requests-for-startups-ideas)); Halter raised $220M Series E for AI in livestock ([wellows](https://wellows.com/blog/ai-startups/)). SPECULATION:
  tightening residue regulation + consumer pressure makes verification a procurement
  requirement by 2028.
- **VC / funding / market signal.** YC RFS (agriculture); Halter $220M (ag-AI appetite proven).
- **How it makes money.** Per-shipment verification fee to buyers + SaaS for supplier
  scorecards; data-network effects as more suppliers get scored.
- **Strongest counterargument.** This may be a *hardware/lab-science* problem (spectroscopy,
  assay accuracy) more than a software/agent problem — the hard part is the sensor and the
  chemistry, not the AI. If so, it's outside any software founder's edge and the moat is a lab,
  not a model.
- **Fit note.** Stretch (far). No ag or analytical-chemistry depth; software-agent edge barely
  applies. Brilliant space, wrong founder.
- **Scores.** Unsolvedness 4 · Timing 3 (early) · Funding momentum 3 · Monetization 3 ·
  Market budget 4 · Defensibility-vs-labs 4 (labs won't, but it's a hardware moat).

## 5. Multi-tier semiconductor supply-chain risk & export-compliance tracking

- **Problem.** A chip buyer or fab managing advanced packaging runs ~1,400 process steps across
  a dozen countries over five months — on spreadsheets and SAP. They have no real-time view of
  multi-tier allocation, single-points-of-failure, or export-control exposure (US-China
  controls, NDAA sourcing). HBM is booked through 2026; a missed sub-tier dependency stalls
  billions in compute ([YC via search](https://www.thevccorner.com/p/yc-summer-2026-requests-for-startups-ideas)).
- **Industry & who pays.** Semiconductors / hardware supply chain. Budget holders: fabs,
  fabless designers, hyperscaler hardware teams, OEMs under NDAA pressure.
- **Why unsolved today.** Worked around with manual sourcing analysts and spreadsheets; ERP
  doesn't model sub-tier risk or export compliance. Data is fragmented and partly secret.
- **Why 2028-2029.** OBSERVED: YC Summer 2026 RFS names semiconductor supply-chain software a
  top bet; reshoring + export controls are accelerating ([TNW](https://thenextweb.com/news/yc-summer-2026-rfs-hard-tech-pivot), [US Mfg Report](https://usmanufacturingreport.com/article/americas-industrial-rebuild-is-accelerating-across-defense-energy-and-ai/)). SPECULATION: by 2028
  export-compliance becomes a board-level, audited function needing live tooling.
- **VC / funding / market signal.** Explicit YC RFS category; $1T+ hyperscaler capex depends on
  this supply chain.
- **How it makes money.** Enterprise SaaS + compliance-evidence module (audit-priced); land via
  one fab/OEM, expand across tiers.
- **Strongest counterargument.** Data access is the whole game and it's guarded/classified;
  without supplier cooperation the graph is empty. SAP/Palantir-class incumbents and primes
  are better-positioned to assemble it, and it's a years-long enterprise sales grind.
- **Fit note.** Stretch. The "compliance-evidence for a regulated workflow" framing touches his
  audit/evals instinct, but no hardware supply-chain domain or network.
- **Scores.** Unsolvedness 4 · Timing 4 · Funding momentum 3 · Monetization 4 · Market budget 5
  · Defensibility-vs-labs 4.

## 6. AI-native upskilling-to-placement engine for hard-tech / skilled-trades labor (service-as-software for the workforce gap)

- **Problem.** The industrial pivot (fabs, drone lines, grid build-out, reshored
  manufacturing) needs technicians, electricians, and trade operators far faster than
  community colleges and apprenticeships produce them. Employers can't hire; workers can't find
  a fast, credible path in. YC named "AI guidance for career training" a Spring 2026 RFS
  ([YC RFS](https://www.ycombinator.com/rfs)).
- **Industry & who pays.** Workforce / vocational training, serving industrial employers.
  Budget holders: manufacturers and government workforce programs (federal AI/industrial spend
  rose sharply — DoE/industrial-policy dollars; gov AI awards up 1,912% to $91.8B potential
  [Brookings](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/where-does-federal-ai-spending-stand-in-2026/)).
- **Why unsolved today.** Worked around with generic LMS + job boards that don't adapt,
  credential, or place. No agent that personalizes training (a16z's "year of me") *and* closes
  the loop into a verified placement.
- **Why 2028-2029.** OBSERVED: YC Spring 2026 RFS (career training) + a16z personalization
  thesis ([a16z](https://a16z.com/newsletter/big-ideas-2026-part-1/)); the hard-tech RFS pivot *creates* the demand. SPECULATION: by 2028
  the trades-labor shortage is the binding constraint on reshoring, and employers/government
  pay for outcomes.
- **VC / funding / market signal.** Two separate YC RFS lines (career training; the hard-tech
  pivot that drives demand); large federal workforce/industrial budget.
- **How it makes money.** Outcome/placement fee from employers (service-as-software: bill for
  the placed worker, not the seat) + government program contracts.
- **Strongest counterargument.** Placement businesses are low-margin, sales-heavy, and trust-
  gated; the AI is the easy part and edtech is a graveyard of good intentions. A foundation lab
  won't build it, but neither is it defensible — and ROI on training is notoriously hard to
  prove to a CFO.
- **Fit note.** Stretch (far). No edtech/workforce domain; weak reliance on his agent edge.
- **Scores.** Unsolvedness 3 · Timing 3 · Funding momentum 2 · Monetization 3 (placement is
  hard) · Market budget 4 · Defensibility-vs-labs 4.

## Shortlist — top 2 by raw opportunity (ignoring fit)

1. **#1 Interconnection & permitting automation for power projects.** The single most acute,
   highest-budget, most clearly-unautomated bottleneck on the board: compute is being built
   faster than it can be powered, the delay costs are seven figures per project, and capital is
   already flowing to the space (GridCARE $64M, Soma $7M). Service-as-software with a hard,
   quantifiable ROI. Foundation labs are irrelevant here; the only real threat is well-funded
   energy-software incumbents — which reads as a race, not a closed door.

2. **#3 Autonomous insurance-claims adjudication with audit trail.** Largest proven recurring
   budget (millions of claims, 70-80% service-as-software margins per William Blair) with the
   clearest monetization and validated investor appetite (Notch $30M). It's more crowded than
   #1 — a knife fight, not an open field — but the regulated-explainability requirement creates
   a real reliability/audit moat that a thin wrapper can't cross.

## One pick best-matched to me

**#3, narrowed to the reliability-and-audit layer.** Of the six, this is the only one whose
*hard part* is what Aditya has actually shipped: a multi-step autonomous decision in a
regulated domain that must be explainable and defensible on demand — i.e. agent
evals/observability + audit-trail + long-horizon reliability, his sharpest edges. The honest
gap is distribution: he has no insurance-carrier network, so the wedge should be selling the
*assurance/audit layer* to the carriers and insurtechs already deploying claims agents
(adjacent to his agent-reliability theses) rather than building a full carrier-facing
adjudicator cold. Everything else on this list (energy queue, counter-drone, semis, ag-residue,
workforce) is a genuine stretch — great spaces, wrong founder — and is flagged as such.
