## Step 2 — Forecast: the AI-shaped world of 2028-2029

**Observed today (sourced).** The frontier is no longer about a smarter chatbot. OpenAI
is "throwing everything" at building a fully automated AI researcher, with Codex as the
early version ([MIT Tech Review](https://www.technologyreview.com/2026/03/20/1134438/openai-is-throwing-everything-into-building-a-fully-automated-researcher/)).
Anthropic's 2026 roadmap is explicitly about agent *teams* that run autonomously for hours
or days, and managed long-running agents for coding, finance, and law — naming
"multi-agent coordination" and "scaling human-agent oversight" as the top unsolved problems
([Anthropic 2026 Agentic Coding Trends](https://rits.shanghai.nyu.edu/ai/anthropics-2026-agentic-coding-trends-report-from-assistants-to-agent-teams/)).
2026 is being called the breakthrough year for reliable **world models and continual
learning**, with Genie-like physics simulators feeding embodied AI, and NVIDIA shipping
Cosmos/GR00T plus Isaac Sim/Lab as the training-and-eval substrate for humanoids
([NextBigFuture](https://www.nextbigfuture.com/2026/04/2026-is-breakthrough-year-for-reliable-ai-world-models-and-continual-learning-prototypes.html)).
Capital is validating verticalization, not horizontal chat: Sierra raised $950M at $15B,
Legora $550M at $5.55B into real-estate/healthcare verticals, and Fieldguide $75M at $700M
for AI-native audit ([Crunchbase](https://news.crunchbase.com/venture/biggest-funding-rounds-sierra-astrani-anagram-therapeutics/);
[Fortune/Fieldguide](https://fortune.com/2026/02/02/goldman-sachs-fieldguide-accounting-cpa-ai-software-platform-venture-capital/)).

**The bet (speculation, labeled).** Extrapolating these curves, by 2028-2029 the default
unit of AI work is not a model call but a *fleet*: software agents working in teams,
physical robots working in fleets, and AI "researchers/analysts/advisors" producing outputs
that enter permanent records (the scientific literature, audited financial statements,
investment advice, academic credentials). Capability will be cheap and abundant;
*trust in the output* will be the bottleneck. Three forces compound: (1) outputs become
consequential and irreversible (a robot acts on the floor, a citation enters the literature,
an audit gets signed, advice moves a client's money); (2) regulators arrive on a fixed
schedule (SEC AI exam priorities live now, Colorado AI Act June 30 2026, California ADMT
Jan 1 2027); and (3) the human reviewers who used to be the safety net are removed by the
very automation that creates the volume.

**Derived problem spaces.** The world of 2028-2029 therefore creates a recurring, fundable
need that barely exists today: **independent assurance that an autonomous system's output is
trustworthy before it becomes irreversible** — across physical fleets, science, regulated
professional work, credentials, and money. Each domain below is a different instance of that
same gap, in a different industry, with a different budget holder. The thread that unites
them — and that maps to where reliability is the moat — is *verification of long-horizon,
high-stakes autonomous output*, which is precisely the layer that doesn't get built by the
labs because it must be independent of them.

## 1. Embodied-agent reliability & pre-deployment validation for robot fleets

**Problem.** A logistics or manufacturing operator about to put a fleet of humanoid/mobile
robots on the floor has no defensible, continuous way to prove each unit will hit the
95–99% uptime and safety bar the line requires — before deployment and after every model/
policy update. Today they run a vendor demo and a pilot and hope. Failures are physical,
per-shift, and severe (a stalled unit halts a line; an unsafe action injures a worker).

**Industry & who pays.** Robotics / advanced manufacturing / warehouse logistics. Budget
holder: the plant or fulfillment operator (and the robot OEM that must clear acceptance
testing to close the sale). This is capex-grade spend — a single private-5G floor alone runs
$150K–$500K ([theresarobotforthat](https://theresarobotforthat.com/blog/humanoid-robot-challenges/)).

**Why unsolved today.** No humanoid-specific safety standard exists yet — ISO 25785-1 is
still a Working Draft expected 2026–2027, so inspectors fall back to generic ISO 10218 plus
manufacturer specs ([safety standards](https://www.theresarobotforthat.com/blog/humanoid-robot-safety-standards-2026/)).
Sim platforms (Isaac Sim, MuJoCo, Gazebo) train policies but don't *certify a deployed
fleet's* reliability in the customer's real environment. Worked around with manual pilots and
the OEM's own (non-independent) testing.

**Why 2028-2029.** SIGNAL: world models + Isaac Lab make high-fidelity scenario generation
real now; pilots with Hyundai/NEURA are explicitly to "evaluate operational reliability and
safety" ([axis-intelligence](https://axis-intelligence.com/humanoid-robots-deployment-2026-case-studies/)).
SPECULATION: as fleets scale past pilots into thousands of units by 2028, an *independent*
validation-and-monitoring layer becomes a purchasing prerequisite, the way crash-test and
SOC2 became gates. Must be true: robots actually reach production volume (uptime/battery are
still blockers today).

**VC / funding / market signal.** a16z American Dynamism's Big Ideas 2026 explicitly calls
for an "AI-native, software-first industrial base… starting with simulation, automated
design, and AI-driven operations" ([a16z Big Ideas 2026](https://a16z.com/newsletter/big-ideas-2026-part-1/)).
Rebar raised $14M Series A for an AI OS for the trades supply chain ([Crunchbase](https://news.crunchbase.com/venture/biggest-funding-rounds-sierra-astrani-anagram-therapeutics/)).
The validation/assurance slice specifically is near-unfunded.

**How it makes money.** Per-fleet/per-robot SaaS for continuous reliability scoring +
per-deployment paid validation runs (service-as-software: deliver the acceptance-test
report, not just a dashboard). Sales motion: land via robot OEMs needing to clear customer
acceptance, expand to operators.

**Strongest counterargument.** The OEMs (Figure, Agility, Boston Dynamics) will treat
validation as core IP and build it in-house, and customers may trust the OEM's own numbers in
an immature market with no independent-audit norm yet. Physical-world data access is also a
brutal cold-start. Timing risk: if fleets stay stuck in pilots through 2028, the buyer isn't
there yet.

**Fit note.** Stretch. The reliability-of-autonomous-systems thesis is exactly his edge, but
the physical/embodied domain is outside his software-agent depth and network — interesting but
not a natural wheelhouse.

**Scores.** Unsolvedness 4 (no independent layer exists) · Timing 2 (fleets still
pre-scale) · Funding momentum 3 (robotics hot, this slice cold) · Monetization clarity 4
(acceptance gate = clear budget) · Market budget 4 (capex-grade) · Defensibility-vs-labs 4
(labs don't do physical-fleet audit).

## 2. Verification & reproducibility layer for AI-generated science

**Problem.** A pharma R&D group, journal, or research lab using AI to generate hypotheses,
literature reviews, and experimental write-ups has no scalable way to verify the output is
real before it enters a regulatory filing or the permanent scientific record. The failure is
concrete: in the first seven weeks of 2026, 1 in 277 papers had at least one non-existent
reference, and an audit of 2.5M papers found ~146,900 AI-generated fake citations in 2025
alone ([phys.org](https://phys.org/news/2026-05-ai-generated-fake-citations-scientific.html);
[Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-00969-z)). Frontier models still
hallucinate 15–20% on citation tasks, 35–55% on niche/recent topics ([Fortune](https://fortune.com/2026/05/24/ai-hallucinations-scientific-research-authors-medical-journal-treatment/)).

**Industry & who pays.** Scientific R&D / pharma-biotech / academic publishing. Budget
holders: pharma R&D and regulatory-affairs teams (a bad citation in an FDA submission is
expensive), journals/publishers facing an integrity crisis, and research universities.

**Why unsolved today.** "Unverified AI output entering the permanent record" is named as the
core problem, and ScienceDirect is calling for publisher-led, academically-supervised
verification that does not yet exist at scale ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S221462962600191X)).
Worked around with manual reviewer spot-checks (which the volume is now overwhelming) and
single-purpose citation checkers that don't verify *claims*, only reference existence.

**Why 2028-2029.** SIGNAL: OpenAI's automated-researcher push means the *volume* of
AI-generated science is about to explode — "hallucinated discovery" sped up 400% while
verification success dipped ([MIT Tech Review](https://www.technologyreview.com/2026/03/20/1134438/openai-is-throwing-everything-into-building-a-fully-automated-researcher/);
[sakab4ever](https://sakab4ever.com/blog/last-leap-for-ai-why-2026-is-a-turning-point)).
SPECULATION: by 2028-2029 publishers and pharma make automated provenance-and-claim
verification a mandatory gate, the way plagiarism checks (Turnitin/iThenticate) became
mandatory. Must be true: institutions enforce it rather than tolerate the pollution.

**VC / funding / market signal.** No clean RFS names this exact wedge, but it sits inside
a16z Bio+Health's 2026 thesis and the broader "verification of autonomous output" theme;
the academic-integrity literature is actively demanding the tool ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S221462962600191X)).
Early/unproven — flag as a thesis bet, not a proven market.

**How it makes money.** Per-manuscript / per-submission verification API to publishers; seat +
usage licenses to pharma regulatory-affairs and R&D; service-as-software "verified evidence
pack" for filings. Sales motion: land a publisher or a pharma regulatory team as a design
partner.

**Strongest counterargument.** The foundation labs are directly attacking their own
hallucination problem (it's their headline weakness), so the gap may shrink from under you;
and publishers are notoriously slow, cheap buyers who may push verification onto authors for
free rather than pay a vendor. Market may be real but mis-monetized.

**Fit note.** Stretch-to-moderate. The verification/eval-of-autonomous-output muscle is his,
but scientific-domain credibility and publisher distribution are not — he'd be learning the
buyer from scratch.

**Scores.** Unsolvedness 5 (openly unsolved, escalating) · Timing 3 (real now, enforcement
lagging) · Funding momentum 2 (theme hot, wedge unfunded) · Monetization clarity 2 (publishers
are weak payers) · Market budget 3 (pharma yes, publishing no) · Defensibility-vs-labs 2
(labs own the hallucination fight).

## 3. Independent review & sign-off assurance for AI-produced professional-services work

**Problem.** An audit/tax/accounting firm now runs AI that drafts 80%+ of returns and cuts
document-review time 50%+, but a human partner still personally signs and carries the
liability. There is no independent, auditable layer that checks the AI's workpapers — that the
numbers tie, the tax position is defensible, the cited rule is current — before the partner
signs. The reviewer is the bottleneck and the legal exposure.

**Industry & who pays.** Professional services — public accounting, audit, tax. Budget holder:
the firm and its partners (and their malpractice insurers). AI-accounting market projected at
$10.87B in 2026 ([DualEntry](https://www.dualentry.com/blog/ai-in-accounting)).

**Why unsolved today.** The funded incumbents (Fieldguide $75M at $700M; Goldman-led) build
the AI that *does* the work — not the independent layer that *reviews and gates* it
([Fortune](https://fortune.com/2026/02/02/goldman-sachs-fieldguide-accounting-cpa-ai-software-platform-venture-capital/)).
Greylock's Call for Startups explicitly lists "vertical AI for professional services in legal,
financial services, accounting and tax" plus "testing and monitoring AI applications" as
separate asks ([Greylock](https://greylock.com/firm-news/call-for-startups/)). Today firms
work around it with manual partner review and sampling — which collapses as AI output volume
rises.

**Why 2028-2029.** SIGNAL: firms report 30–40% efficiency gains and 80% return automation
*now* ([Thomson Reuters](https://tax.thomsonreuters.com/blog/state-of-ai-in-audit/)).
SPECULATION: by 2028-2029, as the producing-AI saturates, the binding constraint flips to
*assured review*, and audit-quality regulators (PCAOB-style) start asking how AI workpapers
were checked. Must be true: liability stays with the human signer (it will).

**VC / funding / market signal.** Greylock's RFS names the adjacent slices directly
([Greylock](https://greylock.com/firm-news/call-for-startups/)); Fieldguide's raise validates
the buyer has money and is spending ([Fortune](https://fortune.com/2026/02/02/goldman-sachs-fieldguide-accounting-cpa-ai-software-platform-venture-capital/)).

**How it makes money.** Per-engagement / per-workpaper review pricing (service-as-software:
deliver a reviewed-and-flagged workpaper set + sign-off evidence), or seat licenses for review
partners. Sales motion: land mid-market CPA firms drowning in AI output without the partner
bandwidth to review it.

**Strongest counterargument.** The producing-AI vendors (Fieldguide et al.) will bundle
"self-review" into their own product and argue you don't need an independent checker —
classic "the tool that does the work also grades the work," which buyers may accept to avoid a
second vendor. Independence is a real value prop only if regulators or insurers force it, and
they haven't yet.

**Fit note.** Stretch. Strong on the reliability/verification thesis and the regulated-buyer
shape he likes, but accounting/audit is a domain and network he has zero edge in.

**Scores.** Unsolvedness 4 (review layer absent) · Timing 4 (producing-AI live now) · Funding
momentum 4 (Greylock RFS + Fieldguide) · Monetization clarity 4 (per-engagement budget
exists) · Market budget 4 ($10B+ market) · Defensibility-vs-labs 3 (vertical incumbents, not
labs, are the threat).

## 4. Verified-skill assessment integrity for an AI-tutor world

**Problem.** Universities, certification bodies, and employers can no longer trust that a
credential reflects a human's actual skill, because AI tutors/assistants do the work
invisibly. There is no scalable way to *prove a person actually learned/can do X* when every
assessment is trivially AI-completable. The failure is per-credential and erodes the entire
signaling value of degrees and certs.

**Industry & who pays.** Education / professional credentialing / HR. Budget holders:
universities and certification bodies (their credential is their product), and employers who
pay to verify hires. Education has the lowest AI adoption (~34%) yet is being hit hardest by
AI's disruption of assessment ([codewave](https://codewave.com/insights/ai-adoption-industry-trends-insights/)).

**Why unsolved today.** Proctoring/anti-cheat tools detect *AI text*, not *whether the human
can do the skill unaided* — a losing arms race as models improve. a16z's consumer thesis
assumes AI tutors become ubiquitous ([a16z Big Ideas 2026](https://a16z.com/newsletter/big-ideas-2026-part-1/)),
which makes the verification gap structural, not temporary. Worked around with in-person
exams (expensive, doesn't scale) and ignoring the problem.

**Why 2028-2029.** SIGNAL: AI tutors are an explicit a16z 2026 bet and adoption is climbing.
SPECULATION: by 2028-2029 the credentialing crisis forces a new standard — adaptive,
oral/interactive, AI-administered-but-AI-resistant assessment that certifies *capability* not
*output*. Must be true: institutions value their credential enough to pay to defend it (some
will, some won't — a real risk).

**VC / funding / market signal.** Weaker/earlier than the others — no major RFS names it
directly; the signal is the structural collapse of assessment under ubiquitous AI tutors
([a16z](https://a16z.com/newsletter/big-ideas-2026-part-1/)) plus documented underserved-market
demand in AI education ([sabrina.dev](https://www.sabrina.dev/p/3-underserved-markets-ai-education)).
Label: early, unproven.

**How it makes money.** Per-assessment / per-credential pricing to certification bodies and
universities; B2B verification API to employers. Sales motion: land a high-stakes
certification body (where credential integrity is existential) as design partner.

**Strongest counterargument.** Education is a notoriously slow, low-budget, fragmented buyer,
and the incentive is perverse — schools may quietly prefer *not* to discover that their
graduates can't do the work. Employers may route around credentials entirely (work samples,
trials) rather than pay for verified ones, killing the market from the other side.

**Fit note.** Stretch. Outside his domain and network; the adversarial-assessment design has
a faint echo of red-teaming but the buyer and motion are foreign to him.

**Scores.** Unsolvedness 4 (structural, unsolved) · Timing 2 (early) · Funding momentum 2
(thin) · Monetization clarity 2 (weak/fragmented buyers) · Market budget 2 (education is
cheap) · Defensibility-vs-labs 3 (labs build tutors, not the integrity layer).

## 5. Suitability & supervisory-review automation for agentic wealth advice

**Problem.** An RIA or wealth platform deploying AI to generate client recommendations must,
by law, keep a human supervisory review in the loop and document that every AI-assisted
recommendation was reviewed for suitability/fiduciary fit before it reaches the client. Doing
this manually doesn't scale; skipping it is an SEC finding. 44% of adviser firms using AI have
*no formal testing or validation* of outputs ([wealthmanagement.com](https://www.wealthmanagement.com/regulation-compliance/sec-2026-examination-priorities-what-financial-services-firms-need-to-know)).

**Industry & who pays.** Financial services / wealth management. Budget holder: RIAs, broker-
dealers, and wealth platforms — financial services already spends 2.6× the cross-industry
average on AI ($3,200/employee) ([codewave](https://codewave.com/insights/ai-adoption-industry-trends-insights/)).

**Why unsolved today.** The SEC's 2026 exam priorities explicitly name AI governance, vendor
oversight, and "documentation showing AI-assisted recommendations are subject to human
supervisory review before they reach clients," with a smaller-firm compliance deadline of
June 3 2026 ([wealthmanagement.com](https://www.wealthmanagement.com/regulation-compliance/sec-2026-examination-priorities-what-financial-services-firms-need-to-know)).
The tooling to *automate that review-and-evidence loop at scale* barely exists — firms hand-
write Acceptable-Use Policies and do ad-hoc review. Greylock's RFS lists "AI agents for
financial services compliance" as a direct ask ([Greylock](https://greylock.com/firm-news/call-for-startups/)).

**Why 2028-2029.** SIGNAL: the regulatory schedule is live now and the SEC is actively asking
RIAs about AI governance ([wealthmanagement.com](https://www.wealthmanagement.com/artificial-intelligence/sec-examiners-are-asking-rias-about-ai-governance-now)).
SPECULATION: as agentic advice scales 2028-2029, the suitability-review layer becomes a
standing operational system (like trade surveillance), not a policy doc. Must be true:
regulators keep requiring human-in-the-loop (they're technology-neutral and show no sign of
relaxing — [Kitces](https://www.kitces.com/blog/artificial-intelligence-compliance-considerations-investment-advisers-sec-securities-exchange-commission-legal-regulation-framework/)).

**VC / funding / market signal.** Greylock RFS names "AI agents for financial services
compliance" outright ([Greylock](https://greylock.com/firm-news/call-for-startups/)); the SEC
deadline is dated and forces budget ([wealthmanagement.com](https://www.wealthmanagement.com/regulation-compliance/sec-2026-examination-priorities-what-financial-services-firms-need-to-know)).

**How it makes money.** Per-adviser-seat SaaS + per-recommendation review/evidence pricing
(service-as-software: produce the audit-ready supervisory-review log). Sales motion: land
mid-size RIAs ahead of an SEC exam; expand to platforms.

**Strongest counterargument.** Compliance incumbents (the trade-surveillance and RIA-compliance
vendors already inside these firms) will bolt an "AI review" module onto their existing
contract and out-distribute a startup — this is a feature land-grab in a buyer that hates
adding vendors. The regulatory ask may also be satisfiable with cheap document templates, not
a real-time system, capping willingness to pay.

**Fit note.** Moderate-to-stretch. Fintech is a buyer type he has some credibility with
(BugBase served HDFC/Groww), and "human-in-the-loop reliability evidence for regulated
autonomous output" is squarely his thesis — but securities-compliance domain depth is missing.

**Scores.** Unsolvedness 4 (tooling absent) · Timing 5 (dated SEC deadline now) · Funding
momentum 3 (Greylock RFS; compliance crowding) · Monetization clarity 4 (regulation forces
budget) · Market budget 5 (highest-spend sector) · Defensibility-vs-labs 4 (regulatory moat;
threat is compliance incumbents, not labs).

## 6. Coordination-failure reliability for multi-agent teams (the "team QA" layer)

**Problem.** A company running a *team* of agents on a long-horizon workflow (research →
plan → code → review, or analyst pods) hits failure modes that single-agent tooling can't
see: deadlock, two agents undoing each other's work, redundant duplicate effort, a wrong
assumption cascading across the team, and silent stalls. The on-call engineer gets per-agent
traces but no view of the *team's* coordination health, and no way to gate a team's output
before it ships. This happens per-run, at scale across many concurrent teams.

**Industry & who pays.** Agent infrastructure / AI-native software. Budget holder: any
enterprise eng/product org deploying multi-agent systems — and the new "agent team" platforms
themselves. (This is distinct from prior digests' single-agent eval, observability, memory,
kill-switch, and traffic-governance ideas: the unit here is the *team*, and the failure is
*inter-agent coordination*.)

**Why unsolved today.** Anthropic names "mastering multi-agent coordination" and "scaling
human-agent oversight" as the two hardest unsolved priorities for 2026, and is itself shipping
agent teams that run for hours/days ([Anthropic 2026 report](https://rits.shanghai.nyu.edu/ai/anthropics-2026-agentic-coding-trends-report-from-assistants-to-agent-teams/)).
a16z calls agent-speed workloads "recursive, bursty, and massive" — i.e., the topology where
coordination failures live ([a16z Big Ideas 2026](https://a16z.com/newsletter/big-ideas-2026-part-1/)).
Today teams work around it with bespoke logging and manual trace-reading.

**Why 2028-2029.** SIGNAL: the shift from single agents to agent teams is the explicit 2026
roadmap of the labs themselves ([Anthropic](https://rits.shanghai.nyu.edu/ai/anthropics-2026-agentic-coding-trends-report-from-assistants-to-agent-teams/)).
SPECULATION: by 2028-2029, multi-agent teams are the default deployment, and coordination-
reliability tooling becomes as standard as APM (Datadog) is for microservices. Must be true:
teams-of-agents actually win over monolithic single agents (plausible but not certain — a
single more-capable agent could absorb the work).

**VC / funding / market signal.** "The most consequential advancement of early 2026 is not
model intelligence but agent infrastructure" ([WhatLLM via search](https://www.bvp.com/atlas/ai-infrastructure-roadmap-five-frontiers-for-2026));
Bessemer's 2026 AI-infra roadmap and a16z's agent-speed thesis both point at this layer
([Bessemer](https://www.bvp.com/atlas/ai-infrastructure-roadmap-five-frontiers-for-2026);
[a16z](https://a16z.com/newsletter/big-ideas-2026-part-1/)). Funded thin as a *coordination-
specific* layer.

**How it makes money.** Usage-based + per-seat SaaS, OSS-core to enterprise (open the team-
tracing SDK, charge for the hosted control plane and gating). Sales motion: founder-led to
technical buyers / AI-agent companies — his exact distribution.

**Strongest counterargument.** This is the most likely to be eaten by a frontier lab or the
agent-framework owners: whoever ships the orchestration runtime (Anthropic, LangChain-style
frameworks, the cloud agent platforms) sees the coordination layer as *theirs* and will bundle
team-level observability into the runtime for free. Winning requires being framework-neutral
and faster than the platforms — a knife fight, not an open field.

**Fit note.** **Best fit on the board.** This sits dead-center on his sharpest edge —
long-horizon agent reliability, evals/observability, multi-provider routing — sold founder-led
to AI-agent companies he can reach. OSS-to-enterprise motion matches Pentest Copilot's
playbook.

**Scores.** Unsolvedness 4 (team-level gap is real) · Timing 4 (labs shipping teams now) ·
Funding momentum 4 (agent-infra is the hottest infra theme) · Monetization clarity 3 (infra
monetization is real but competitive) · Market budget 3 (early, growing fast) ·
Defensibility-vs-labs 2 (highest lab/framework-encroachment risk on the list).

## Shortlist — top 2 by raw opportunity (ignoring fit)

1. **#5 Suitability & supervisory-review automation for agentic wealth advice.** Best
opportunity on raw merits: a *dated* regulatory forcing function (SEC exam priorities live;
June 3 2026 deadline), the highest-spending sector, a named Greylock RFS, and a defensible
regulatory moat that the foundation labs have no interest in occupying. The risk is incumbent
compliance vendors, not labs — a winnable fight if entered fast.

2. **#3 Independent review & sign-off assurance for AI-produced professional-services work.**
Rises for the cleanest budget-meets-gap story: the producing-AI is already live and funded
(Fieldguide), Greylock's RFS names the adjacent slices, partners carry personal liability that
won't transfer to a machine, and the $10B+ market is spending today. The catch — independence
only becomes a *requirement* if regulators/insurers force it — is the one thing to validate
first.

## One pick best-matched to me

**#6 Coordination-failure reliability for multi-agent teams.** It is the only idea that sits
exactly on his sharpest, demonstrated edge — making long-horizon agents work in production —
with an OSS-to-enterprise motion identical to Pentest Copilot and a buyer (AI-agent companies)
he can actually reach. The honest caveat is also the reason it's not the top *raw*
opportunity: defensibility against the labs/frameworks is the weakest on the board, so the
wedge has to be a sharp, framework-neutral reliability primitive (coordination-failure
detection + team-level output gating) that the platforms won't prioritize — not a general
"agent ops" play. If he wants founder-fit, this is the one; if he wants the cleanest market,
#5 is the bet.
