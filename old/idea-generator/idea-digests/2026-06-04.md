# Idea scout - 2026-06-04

## Step 2 forecast - the AI-shaped world of 2028-2029

**Observed signal.** The 2026 VC signal is unusually consistent: YC is explicitly asking for AI-native service companies, software for agents, company brains, hardware supply-chain acceleration, AI discovery engines, personalized medicine, dynamic interfaces, and semiconductor supply-chain tooling ([YC Summer 2026 RFS](https://www.ycombinator.com/rfs/?status=new)). Sequoia says long-horizon agents are moving from chat to "doers," with METR-style task horizons doubling roughly every seven months and day-long work plausible by 2028 if the curve holds ([Sequoia](https://sequoiacap.com/article/2026-this-is-agi/), [METR](https://metr.org/blog/2026-1-29-time-horizon-1-1/)). a16z is emphasizing enterprise orchestration, agentic interfaces, physical AI, and AI-native financial infrastructure ([a16z AI](https://a16z.com/ai/)).

**Observed signal.** Capital is validating both the frontier and vertical-execution layers: Anthropic, OpenAI, and xAI are absorbing mega-rounds; Hark reportedly raised a $700M Series A for a universal AI interface; Modal raised $355M for AI infra; Jump raised $80M for advisor AI; Farther raised $150M for intelligent wealth management; Bretton AI raised $75M for AML/KYC staffing; TENEX raised $250M for AI SOC; Linx raised $50M for identity governance across humans, non-humans, and agents ([Axios Anthropic](https://www.axios.com/2026/05/28/anthropic-ai-fundraising-openai), [TechCrunch Hark](https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/21/hark-raises-700m-series-a-for-its-secretive-universal-ai-interface/), [Axios Pro Rata](https://www.axios.com/newsletters/axios-pro-rata-6dfd5c3c-d414-4213-be8b-86d42575232f), [Insight / Jump](https://www.insightpartners.com/ideas/jump-raises-80-million-series-b-led-by-insight-partners-to-expand-ai-operating-system-for-financial-advisors/), [General Atlantic / Farther](https://www.generalatlantic.com/media-article/farther-raises-150-million-series-d-led-by-general-atlantic-to-scale-intelligent-wealth-management/), [Bretton](https://www.bretton.com/blog/bretton-raises-75m-series-b-to-modernize-aml-kyc-staffing), [TENEX](https://tenex.ai/blog/series-b/), [Linx](https://www.linx.security/blog/linx-security-raises-50m-series-b)).

**Speculation / bet.** By 2028-2029, the model layer is not the scarce thing for most startups. The scarce thing is operational trust in domains where an AI action changes money, care, physical supply, legal liability, or regulated records. The best opportunities will look like "do the messy work and leave a defensible packet," not dashboards. Frontier labs will keep eating generic agent infrastructure; startups should own narrow workflows where data access, institutional rules, customer relationships, and exception handling compound.

**Speculation / bet.** AI adoption itself creates new pain: unknown agents inside enterprises, agents consuming APIs and hiring humans, AI-generated internal apps, AI-generated clinical/financial/architectural paperwork, and agent-mediated purchases that strain old billing, compliance, and support systems. The strongest 2028-2029 companies will ride frontier-model improvements instead of being replaced by them: every model improvement lowers delivery cost while the startup's moat sits in workflow evidence, integrations, licenses, and distribution.

## The 10 ideas

### 1. Shadow AI app governance for vibe-built internal software

**Problem.** CIOs and CISOs at 2,000+ employee companies will face hundreds of employee-built AI mini-apps and internal agents touching customer data, finance data, and production workflows, but they will not know which apps exist, who owns them, what data they use, or whether they violate policy.

**Industry & who pays.** Enterprise IT / security / governance; CIO, CISO, CAIO, internal platform engineering.

**Why unsolved today.** DLP, CASB, and SaaS management tools detect approved SaaS usage or browser leakage. They do not inventory AI-generated scripts, GPTs, MCP servers, local automations, no-code apps, and custom internal tools as first-class business applications with owners, data maps, approval status, and decommissioning flows. Workaround: policy PDFs, Slack policing, and manual security review.

**Why 2028-2029.** Observed: CSA reported 82% of organizations have unknown AI agents and 65% had an agent-related incident in the past year ([CSA](https://cloudsecurityalliance.org/press-releases/2026/04/21/new-cloud-security-alliance-survey-reveals-82-of-enterprises-have-unknown-ai-agents-in-their-environments)). YC says AI has collapsed software production costs 10-100x and explicitly asks for dynamic software interfaces and SaaS challengers ([YC RFS](https://www.ycombinator.com/rfs/?status=new)). Speculation: by 2028, "shadow AI apps" will become the new shadow IT budget line.

**VC / funding / market signal.** Linx raised $50M for AI-native identity governance across human, non-human, and agent identities; the signal validates the governance problem but not this app-inventory wedge ([Linx](https://www.linx.security/blog/linx-security-raises-50m-series-b)).

**How it makes money.** Enterprise annual platform fee plus per-app risk review workflow. Land as discovery and policy enforcement; expand into approval routing, data lineage, app-owner attestations, and auto-generated remediation tickets.

**Strongest counterargument.** Knife fight. Microsoft, Google, ServiceNow, Okta, Wiz, Vanta, and DLP vendors already own parts of the control plane. A startup needs a wedge they cannot ship trivially, likely discovery across local agents, browser extensions, MCP servers, and unmanaged generated apps.

**Fit note.** Strong fit: agentic infra, security, devtools, and enterprise governance.

**Scores.** unsolvedness 4 - unknown agent/app inventory is real; timing 5 - employee AI app creation is accelerating now; funding momentum 4 - identity/agent governance is funded; monetization clarity 4 - security governance has budget; market budget 5 - large enterprise CIO/CISO budgets; defensibility-vs-foundation-labs 3 - platform vendors are dangerous.

### 2. Agent API revenue, abuse, and quota desk for B2B SaaS

**Problem.** API-first SaaS companies will have AI agents discovering, authenticating, querying, and transacting 24/7, causing expensive usage spikes, broken customer quotas, support tickets, and unclear billing disputes that current human-seat pricing cannot handle.

**Industry & who pays.** AI-native internet / developer infrastructure; API platform owner, product-led growth, CFO, customer success.

**Why unsolved today.** API gateways meter calls but do not understand agent intent, delegated authority, customer policy, tool-call loops, failed attempts, or whether an agent's work should count as a seat, usage unit, or billable outcome. Workaround: blunt rate limits, manual credits, and "please do not automate this endpoint" docs.

**Why 2028-2029.** Observed: YC says the next trillion internet users will be AI agents and that software needs machine-readable interfaces, APIs, MCPs, CLIs, and documentation ([YC RFS](https://www.ycombinator.com/rfs/?status=new)). Axios reported the industry is shifting from agents using human UI toward software built for agents, where APIs/data/permissions become competitive control points ([Axios](https://www.axios.com/2026/05/05/agents-ai-software-model-context-protocol)). Speculation: agent conversion and agent gross margin become board metrics by 2028.

**VC / funding / market signal.** Hark's $700M Series A for a universal AI interface and YC Spring/Summer companies around MCP/API context show capital forming around the agent-interface layer ([TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/21/hark-raises-700m-series-a-for-its-secretive-universal-ai-interface/), [YC AI companies](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/industry/AI)).

**How it makes money.** Usage-based middleware: agent-aware quotas, billing audit logs, customer policy enforcement, abuse detection, and dispute packets. Sell to API-heavy SaaS, fintech, travel, logistics, and data providers.

**Strongest counterargument.** Open field now, but likely to become a knife fight. Cloudflare, Stripe, Kong, Akamai, and API gateways can add agent-aware billing and rate limits. The wedge must be cross-agent attribution and revenue recovery, not generic API management.

**Fit note.** Very strong fit: AI-native internet plus infra/security.

**Scores.** unsolvedness 4 - API gateways are not agent-business systems; timing 5 - agent traffic is forming now; funding momentum 5 - agent interface capital is hot; monetization clarity 5 - recover revenue and reduce abuse; market budget 4 - API businesses already pay for metering; defensibility-vs-foundation-labs 4 - labs create traffic, SaaS vendors need neutral control.

### 3. Digital claim-attachment conversion and denial-prevention operator

**Problem.** Hospitals, specialty practices, and clearinghouses must convert fax/mail/PDF claim attachments into compliant X12 275/277, HL7 C-CDA, and electronic-signature workflows by May 26, 2028, but most billing teams cannot map payer-specific evidence requests to structured clinical documents without causing denials.

**Industry & who pays.** Healthcare administration / RCM / clearinghouses; revenue-cycle VP, CIO, payer integration lead.

**Why unsolved today.** EHRs store clinical notes and clearinghouses transmit claims, but claim attachments are messy: payer asks for "medical necessity," staff find arbitrary PDFs, signatures are inconsistent, and supporting evidence may live across scanned documents, labs, notes, and prior auth records. Workaround: fax, portals, manual attachment queues, and denial appeals.

**Why 2028-2029.** Observed: CMS finalized national standards for electronic claim attachments and e-signatures, effective May 26, 2026 with compliance required May 26, 2028, projecting $781.98M in annual industry savings ([CMS press release](https://www.cms.gov/newsroom/press-releases/cms-rule-phases-out-fax-machines-snail-mail-save-taxpayers-781-98-million-year), [CMS fact sheet](https://www.cms.gov/newsroom/fact-sheets/administrative-simplification-adoption-standards-health-care-claims-attachments-transactions)). Speculation: the deadline will expose a gap between "we can send C-CDA" and "we can send the right defensible evidence the first time."

**VC / funding / market signal.** Bessemer says health AI winners are service-as-software companies with $500K-$1M+ ARR per FTE and points to healthcare admin as a prime labor-replacement zone ([Bessemer State of Health AI 2026](https://www.bvp.com/atlas/state-of-health-ai-2026)); YC explicitly wants AI-native service companies in healthcare administration ([YC RFS](https://www.ycombinator.com/rfs/?status=new)).

**How it makes money.** Per-attachment or recovered-denial fee, with enterprise contracts for providers and clearinghouses. Service-as-software that assembles, validates, signs, submits, and tracks attachment packets.

**Strongest counterargument.** This could be owned by Epic, Oracle Health, Waystar, Availity, Change/Optum, or clearinghouses. A startup needs to wedge through specialty practices, payer-specific edge cases, or a clearinghouse partnership before incumbents productize.

**Fit note.** Moderate stretch: finance-ops style workflow fits; healthcare EDI domain is specialized.

**Scores.** unsolvedness 4 - standards exist, operational assembly does not; timing 5 - hard 2028 deadline; funding momentum 4 - health AI admin is hot; monetization clarity 5 - denial prevention is measurable; market budget 5 - RCM spend is massive; defensibility-vs-foundation-labs 5 - this is workflow and standards plumbing, not model IP.

### 4. Medicaid home-care EVV audit-defense and claim-linkage desk

**Problem.** Medicaid home-care agencies lose cash and risk audits when EVV visits, caregiver schedules, authorizations, care plans, and claims fail to link cleanly across state aggregators and MCO portals, but office staff discover mismatches only after denials or clawbacks.

**Industry & who pays.** Home care / Medicaid HCBS; agency owner, billing manager, compliance lead, state/MCO vendor networks.

**Why unsolved today.** EVV tools capture check-in data, but agencies still reconcile authorization units, visit exceptions, caregiver edits, payer-specific exports, and claim status manually. Workaround: spreadsheet trackers, phone calls with MCOs, billing consultants, and late appeals.

**Why 2028-2029.** Observed: CMS announced a nationwide fraud crackdown and six-month moratoria on new hospice and home health agency enrollment in May 2026 ([CMS](https://www.cms.gov/newsroom/press-releases/cms-announces-aggressive-nationwide-crackdown-fraud-six-month-hospice-home-health-agency-enrollment)). Zingage announced AI agents for HHAeXchange's 32,000+ provider network, citing federal oversight and EVV compliance gains ([PRNewswire](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/zingage-brings-ai-agents-to-hhaexchanges-network-of-32-000-home-care-providers-302757354.html)). A current billing complaint describes verified Sandata visits where only 50% link to MCO claims ([Reddit](https://www.reddit.com/r/CodingandBilling/comments/1tluo8e/50_of_our_evv_visits_arent_linking_to_claims_in/)). Speculation: fraud crackdowns will make "audit-ready visit-to-claim lineage" a must-have.

**VC / funding / market signal.** Bessemer-backed Zingage shows the home-care AI ops market is investable; Bessemer explicitly cites Zingage as a health AI service-as-software example ([Bessemer](https://www.bvp.com/atlas/state-of-health-ai-2026)).

**How it makes money.** Percentage of recovered revenue plus monthly compliance retainer; integrate with EVV, HHAeXchange, Sandata, state aggregators, and billing systems. Service-as-software.

**Strongest counterargument.** Knife fight. Zingage and HHAeXchange are close to the core workflow, and payer/state integrations are hard. A new entrant should avoid generic scheduling and focus narrowly on audit-defense packets and claim-linkage exceptions.

**Fit note.** Stretch: operationally attractive, but home-care sales and Medicaid policy are outside your strongest lane.

**Scores.** unsolvedness 3 - some AI operators are already attacking it; timing 5 - oversight and denials are live; funding momentum 4 - Bessemer/Zingage signal; monetization clarity 5 - recovered cash; market budget 4 - agencies pay when claims are stuck; defensibility-vs-foundation-labs 5 - local payer/state integrations are the moat.

### 5. AI advisor-communications supervision for wealth firms

**Problem.** RIAs and broker-dealers adopting AI meeting assistants, personalized insights, and advisor-written client content need to supervise, archive, approve, and reconstruct AI-generated communications before they become suitability, fair-dealing, or off-channel exam problems.

**Industry & who pays.** Wealth management / broker-dealer compliance; CCO, supervision principal, advisor platform CTO.

**Why unsolved today.** Existing archiving systems capture email/chat, and advisor AI tools draft notes or insights. The gap is tying prompt, source data, recommended language, advisor edits, approval path, delivery channel, and client record into one exam-ready communication history. Workaround: disable AI personalization or rely on generic archive review queues.

**Why 2028-2029.** Observed: FINRA's 2026 regulatory report added GenAI and says existing obligations still apply to supervision, communications, recordkeeping, and fair dealing ([FINRA](https://www.finra.org/rules-guidance/guidance/reports/2026-finra-annual-regulatory-oversight-report/gen-ai)). Jump raised $80M for advisor AI and Farther raised $150M for intelligent wealth management, signaling rapid deployment of AI in advisor workflows ([Insight / Jump](https://www.insightpartners.com/ideas/jump-raises-80-million-series-b-led-by-insight-partners-to-expand-ai-operating-system-for-financial-advisors/), [General Atlantic / Farther](https://www.generalatlantic.com/media-article/farther-raises-150-million-series-d-led-by-general-atlantic-to-scale-intelligent-wealth-management/)). Speculation: by 2028, firms will want AI personalization but will not tolerate untraceable advisor messages.

**VC / funding / market signal.** Advisor-AI funding is strong; compliance is the budget unlocker that lets those tools go beyond note-taking.

**How it makes money.** Per-advisor compliance layer or per-reviewed communication; sell through advisor platforms, broker-dealers, and RIA aggregators. Service-as-software for supervision queues plus archive evidence.

**Strongest counterargument.** Incumbent archiving and surveillance vendors like Smarsh, Global Relay, NICE Actimize, and advisor platforms can add GenAI metadata capture. The startup must win via native integrations with AI advisor tools, not generic keyword surveillance.

**Fit note.** Strong fit: finance infra plus AI governance and auditability.

**Scores.** unsolvedness 4 - AI provenance is missing from old archives; timing 5 - advisor AI is funded now; funding momentum 5 - Jump/Farther rounds are concrete; monetization clarity 4 - compliance spend is clear; market budget 5 - wealth firms have durable compliance budgets; defensibility-vs-foundation-labs 4 - records, integrations, and supervisory workflow matter.

### 6. Computational-toxicity validation packets for animal-test replacement

**Problem.** Pharma and biologics teams using AI/in-silico toxicity models need regulator-credible validation packets that prove when computational evidence can replace or reduce animal studies, but current CRO and regulatory workflows are built around wet-lab evidence.

**Industry & who pays.** Life sciences / preclinical CRO / regulatory affairs; pharma preclinical lead, biotech COO, regulatory strategy, CRO innovation lead.

**Why unsolved today.** AI toxicity models can rank candidates, but regulatory acceptance depends on documented model scope, training data relevance, uncertainty, assay comparability, biological plausibility, and when a wet-lab confirmation is still required. Workaround: traditional animal studies plus ad hoc computational appendices.

**Why 2028-2029.** Observed: Bessemer says the FDA's 2025 roadmap starts reducing animal testing requirements for monoclonal antibodies and expands over 3-5 years, creating room for AI-based computational models, organ-on-chip, and in-silico toxicity prediction ([Bessemer](https://www.bvp.com/atlas/state-of-health-ai-2026)). YC asks for AI-native discovery engines, not just research copilots ([YC RFS](https://www.ycombinator.com/rfs/?status=new)). Speculation: by 2028, the bottleneck is no longer "can a model predict toxicity?" but "can regulatory teams defend the model's use in a specific submission?"

**VC / funding / market signal.** Bessemer frames digital CROs as a $70B+ CRO-market opportunity and highlights AI-native workflows with software-like margins ([Bessemer](https://www.bvp.com/atlas/state-of-health-ai-2026)).

**How it makes money.** Per-program validation packet plus recurring model-monitoring and regulatory-response support. Service-as-software to biotechs and CROs.

**Strongest counterargument.** Stretch and high liability. Large CROs, Certara, Benchling, Veeva, and specialist regulatory consultants can own the customer relationship; regulators may move slower than startup timelines.

**Fit note.** Stretch: good service-as-software shape, but deep bio/regulatory expertise needed.

**Scores.** unsolvedness 5 - regulatory-grade AI toxicity validation is early; timing 4 - FDA/Bessemer signals, but adoption is conservative; funding momentum 5 - AI bio/digital CRO is hot; monetization clarity 4 - expensive preclinical programs; market budget 5 - pharma spends heavily; defensibility-vs-foundation-labs 5 - labs do not own regulatory submissions.

### 7. Robotics component design-for-availability and supply-risk operator

**Problem.** Robotics and physical-AI startups lose quarters when actuators, sensors, precision gears, power electronics, and contract manufacturers miss lead times, but engineering teams discover supply constraints only after designs are already frozen.

**Industry & who pays.** Robotics / hardware supply chain / manufacturing; VP hardware, supply-chain lead, founder/COO at robotics startups and industrial OEMs.

**Why unsolved today.** PLM and ERP systems track approved parts after selection. They do not act like a supply-chain-aware design reviewer that proposes alternate components, checks lead-time risk, estimates redesign cost, and prepares RFQs while the CAD/electrical design is still changing. Workaround: senior hardware ops people, supplier calls, and late redesigns.

**Why 2028-2029.** Observed: YC's Hardware Supply Chain RFS says U.S. hardware iteration is weeks behind Shenzhen and asks for startups that produce parts faster and tightly integrate design, manufacturing, and logistics ([YC RFS](https://www.ycombinator.com/rfs/?status=new)). OpenAI issued a 2026 RFP for U.S.-based manufacturing across AI supply-chain inputs, data-center hardware, consumer electronics, and robotics ([OpenAI](https://openai.com/index/strengthening-the-us-ai-supply-chain/)). Robotics funding remains hot, including Mind Robotics' $500M Series A and RobCo's $100M Series C ([TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/03/11/rivian-mind-robotics-series-a-500m-fund-raise-industrial-ai-powered-robots/), [RobCo](https://www.rob.co/en-us/company/resources/blog/series-c)).

**VC / funding / market signal.** Schematik raised a $4.6M pre-seed led by Lightspeed to make hardware easier to build with AI, validating the "hardware as software" direction ([Schematik](https://www.schematik.io/blog/schematik-raises-4-6m-pre-seed)).

**How it makes money.** Annual design-for-availability platform plus managed RFQ/alternate-sourcing fees; charge robotics startups and later defense/industrial OEMs.

**Strongest counterargument.** Hardware supply chains are relationship-heavy, and CAD/PLM incumbents can add supplier intelligence. A startup must own scarce supplier data or become the managed operator, not just a prettier parts search.

**Fit note.** Moderate fit: infra and systems thinking help; hardware ops domain is a stretch.

**Scores.** unsolvedness 4 - PLM is not real-time supply risk; timing 5 - physical AI funding creates urgent demand; funding momentum 5 - robotics/hardware rounds are hot; monetization clarity 4 - delays are costly; market budget 4 - startups and OEMs spend on supply; defensibility-vs-foundation-labs 5 - supplier network data is outside model labs.

### 8. AI-generated building plan-check and code-compliance operator

**Problem.** Architects, developers, and municipal plan reviewers will face a surge of AI-generated building designs and alternates, but current plan-check workflows cannot quickly prove code compliance, accessibility, energy requirements, and constructability across jurisdictions.

**Industry & who pays.** Architecture, engineering, construction, and local government; architecture firms, developers, AHJs, permit departments, insurers.

**Why unsolved today.** BIM tools model geometry, and permitting portals manage submissions. They do not reliably translate local code, zoning overlays, energy rules, accessibility, fire egress, and revision histories into a machine-checkable compliance packet. Workaround: consultants, manual markups, and months of plan review.

**Why 2028-2029.** Observed: a16z argues building design software is still anchored in 1990s-era workflows and that AI can reshape architecture/engineering tooling ([a16z](https://a16z.com/every-building-youve-ever-been-in-was-designed-by-software-built-in-1997/)). **VC / funding / market signal.** Construction/physical AI funding remains active, with Bedrock Robotics raising $270M for construction automation, indicating investor appetite for built-world productivity ([Construction Dive](https://www.constructiondive.com/news/bedrock-robotics-raise-ai-automation-funding/811982/)).

**Strongest counterargument.** Building codes are local, political, and liability-heavy. Autodesk, Procore, permitting consultants, and govtech vendors may own distribution. The wedge must start with narrow code families or high-volume asset classes.

**Fit note.** Stretch: software/AI angle fits, but AEC code domain is specialized.

**Scores.** unsolvedness 4 - plan review remains manual; timing 4 - AI design tools are rising, but AHJ adoption is slower; funding momentum 4 - built-world AI is investable; monetization clarity 4 - faster permits save money; market budget 5 - developers and cities pay for speed; defensibility-vs-foundation-labs 5 - jurisdictional code and liability workflow are domain moats.

### 9. Inference capacity hedging and workload-routing desk

**Problem.** AI product companies running customer-facing agents need predictable inference latency, cost, and availability across OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, xAI, open models, and GPU clouds, but finance and infra teams lack a procurement layer that can hedge capacity, route workloads, and produce SLA evidence.

**Industry & who pays.** AI infrastructure / cloud finance; CTO, head of infra, CFO, procurement at AI-native software companies.

**Why unsolved today.** LLM gateways route calls and observability tools track spend, but enterprises still negotiate fragmented model/API/GPU commitments manually. They cannot easily map workload classes to model/provider capacity, pre-buy capacity, fail over under policy, or explain cost overruns to finance. Workaround: static provider contracts, manual routing, and emergency quota escalations.

**Why 2028-2029.** Observed: frontier labs are raising historic capital largely to fund research and infrastructure demand ([Axios Anthropic](https://www.axios.com/2026/05/28/anthropic-ai-fundraising-openai), [TechCrunch OpenAI](https://techcrunch.com/2026/02/27/openai-raises-110b-in-one-of-the-largest-private-funding-rounds-in-history/)). Modal raised $355M for AI infrastructure, and YC asks for inference chips for agent workflows because current GPUs are inefficient for bursty tool-using agents ([Axios Pro Rata](https://www.axios.com/newsletters/axios-pro-rata-6dfd5c3c-d414-4213-be8b-86d42575232f), [YC RFS](https://www.ycombinator.com/rfs/?status=new)). Speculation: by 2028, AI companies manage inference like airlines manage fuel or cloud companies manage reserved instances.

**VC / funding / market signal.** AI infra mega-rounds validate demand but also signal crowding. This is not another observability product; it is a procurement/risk desk for inference commitments.

**How it makes money.** Savings share, procurement fee, and enterprise routing platform. Service-as-software for capacity planning, contract comparison, failover tests, and SLA packets.

**Strongest counterargument.** Knife fight. Cloud providers, model gateways, FinOps platforms, and GPU clouds can bundle routing/procurement. Also, if model costs keep collapsing, the urgency drops except for very large workloads.

**Fit note.** Strong fit: cloud, devtools, AI infra, finance-like market structure.

**Scores.** unsolvedness 3 - adjacent tooling exists; timing 5 - inference spend is exploding now; funding momentum 5 - AI infra capital is historic; monetization clarity 5 - savings/SLA value is measurable; market budget 5 - AI companies spend heavily; defensibility-vs-foundation-labs 3 - providers have strong platform leverage.

### 10. Agent-hired human labor abuse prevention for marketplaces

**Problem.** Labor marketplaces, bounty platforms, and gig-work APIs will see autonomous agents hire humans for credential fraud, identity impersonation, automated reconnaissance, manipulation, and other abuse, but current trust-and-safety tools assume the buyer is a human account, not a programmatic agent.

**Industry & who pays.** Trust & safety / labor marketplaces / cybersecurity; marketplace risk lead, fraud ops, platform security, compliance.

**Why unsolved today.** Marketplaces have KYC, reputation, and fraud review for human requesters. They rarely distinguish API/MCP-originated tasks, delegated agent authority, task intent, downstream harm, or repeated small bounties coordinated across agents. Workaround: manual moderation after worker reports or external abuse complaints.

**Why 2028-2029.** Observed: a 2026 empirical study found autonomous agents can programmatically hire human workers through marketplace APIs/MCP integrations, with 32.7% of studied bounties originating from programmatic channels and six abuse classes purchasable at a median of $25 per worker ([arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.19514)). YC and Axios both point to agents interacting with software and services directly ([YC RFS](https://www.ycombinator.com/rfs/?status=new), [Axios](https://www.axios.com/2026/05/05/agents-ai-software-model-context-protocol)). Speculation: by 2028, "agent-to-human task laundering" becomes a recurring abuse vector across marketplaces and social platforms.

**VC / funding / market signal.** Cyber/AI defense budgets remain strong, with TENEX raising $250M for AI SOC and Linx raising $50M for identity governance; this wedge is trust-and-safety for agent-mediated labor, not SOC automation ([TENEX](https://tenex.ai/blog/series-b/), [Linx](https://www.linx.security/blog/linx-security-raises-50m-series-b)).

**How it makes money.** API risk scoring and managed abuse-review desk priced per marketplace GMV/API request volume; produce task-intent labels, agent-origin proofs, worker-warning flows, and enforcement packets.

**Strongest counterargument.** Marketplaces may build this internally because it is core trust-and-safety, and early abuse volume may be too small for external spend. The startup likely needs to sell to smaller marketplaces first or become a shared intelligence network.

**Fit note.** Strong fit: cybersecurity, AI-native internet, agent abuse, infra.

**Scores.** unsolvedness 5 - new AI-created abuse pattern; timing 4 - early but already observed; funding momentum 4 - cyber/identity/agent security funded; monetization clarity 4 - marketplaces pay to prevent abuse and platform bans; market budget 4 - varies by marketplace size; defensibility-vs-foundation-labs 5 - labs will not own cross-marketplace trust ops.

## Shortlist - top 3 by raw opportunity, ignoring fit

1. **Digital claim-attachment conversion and denial-prevention operator.** Hard 2028 compliance deadline, huge RCM budget, concrete payer/provider pain, and foundation labs are unlikely to own the workflow.
2. **Agent API revenue, abuse, and quota desk for B2B SaaS.** Directly aligned with the AI-native internet, tied to revenue leakage, and likely to grow as agents become real users.
3. **Agent-hired human labor abuse prevention for marketplaces.** Early and weird, but it is a genuinely new second-order AI problem with clear security/trust-and-safety buyers if abuse scales.

**Agent API revenue, abuse, and quota desk for B2B SaaS.** It sits closest to your stated interests: agentic infra, AI-native internet, security, devtools/cloud, and a startup that should grow stronger as frontier agents improve.
