# Interview design

The quality of what you learn equals the quality of your questions × whether
you're asking the right people. This skill covers both.

## Target profile (who)

Specify job titles, company types, team structure, and seniority of those most
likely to feel the problem *acutely*. Then list where they actually congregate
(subreddits, Slack/Discord, LinkedIn groups, conferences) so they're reachable.
If the hypothesis has multiple personas (e.g. finance manager vs. CFO), design a
SEPARATE framework per persona — one flat guide erases the distinction.

## Question rules (what)

- **Past, not future.** Ask "tell me about the last time you dealt with X", never
  "would you use a tool that…". Stated future intent is near-worthless.
- **Behavior, not opinion.** What they *did*, what it cost, what they tried.
- **Non-leading.** Don't embed the answer or your solution in the question.
- **Open, then specific.** Start broad, then drill into the concrete instance.

## Audit checklist (flag and rewrite)

For every drafted question, flag it if it is:
- **leading** ("don't you hate it when…")
- **future-facing / hypothetical** ("would you…", "could you imagine…")
- **too broad** ("how do you handle operations?")
- **socially-desirable-bait** (invites the flattering answer, not the honest one)

Set `non_leading = false` if any question survives the audit unrewritten.

## Follow-up probes

For the 2–3 moments most likely to produce deflection or vagueness, pre-write a
probe: "what made that hard?", "walk me through exactly what you did next",
"how often does that happen?".
